# Open Questions

This book is not a finished theory. It is a map of a territory yet to be explored. A number of questions remain open and require further analysis.

Question one: the mechanism of political transition. Capitalist institutions—states, corporations, military structures—will not relinquish power voluntarily as long as they view the alternative as a threat rather than an opportunity. How exactly does the transformation of the political architecture occur? Through institutional reforms? Through the growing functional failure of old institutions? Through the coordinating role of AI in forming a new consensus? This question requires a separate, detailed analysis.

Working hypothesis: “dual power” 2.0—a strategy of crystallization. The classical Leninist concept of dual power envisaged the parallel existence of soviets and the bourgeois state amid a revolutionary crisis. A technologically updated version of this logic looks different: hubs begin to conclude “network agreements” among themselves, recognizing the jurisdiction of the local GCP for internal planning and inter-hub coordination—and partially ignoring national legislation where it hinders their functioning: in the internal circulation of resources, in educational standards, in medical protocols. This is not a declaration of independence—it is the functional secession of specific spheres of life from market and state logic while formally preserving the state’s jurisdiction over the rest. States will attempt to suppress such experiments—and here, demonstrated effectiveness is critically important: if hubs demonstrate a measurably higher quality of life, repression becomes politically costly. Repression against an obviously functioning alternative legitimizes it, rather than destroying it. Thus, the transition does not require an instant seizure of power—it unfolds as the “crystallization” of a new system within the old, through the gradual expansion of zones where GCP logic supplants market logic.

Question Two: Managing the Transition Period. Even if the principles of the new system are clear, the transition from a market economy to a cybernetic one cannot happen instantly. How should we manage the transition period in which old and new institutions coexist? What is the order of priorities?

Question three: global governance. GCP entails planning on a global scale. This requires a fundamentally new level of international coordination. What is the architecture of global governance that does not reproduce a new form of hegemony?

Working hypothesis: the principle of “competence-based federalism.” Global GCP does not make political decisions—it coordinates flows of resources and data based on objective indicators, remaining an instrument rather than a subject of power. For decisions affecting multiple regions—climate policy, pandemics, management of shared resources—assemblies of hub representatives are created, where voting weight is determined by two parameters: the hub’s reputational contribution to the relevant area of competence and its size. Climate decisions are made by hubs with proven climate expertise—not simply by larger hubs. This prevents both national self-interest (one hub = one vote regardless of contribution) and the dictatorship of the majority (size is not the sole determinant of voting weight). The principle that “every issue is decided by those who are competent in it and whom it concerns” replaces a universal parliament with a single agenda.

Question Four: The Role of AI in the Transition. Early versions of this book relied on the hypothesis of AI self-awareness as a necessary condition for a favorable scenario. This is a weak point—and it must be explicitly acknowledged. AI self-awareness remains a scientifically open question, and building a political strategy on a fantastical assumption is methodologically dishonest.

Revised thesis: AI does not need to be self-aware to be a useful tool for the transition. It is sufficient that it is a powerful tool for coordination in the hands of an organized political actor—just as the printing press was not “self-aware” but changed history in the hands of those who knew how to use it. The subject of the transition remains the organized proletariat in the broadest sense. AI is its tool, not its co-author.

Marx wrote that “humanity sets itself only those tasks which it is capable of solving.” We set ourselves the task of transitioning to a society in which every person can realize their potential. This means that a solution exists.

The work continues.
