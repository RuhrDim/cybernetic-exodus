# Chapter 5. Scenario One: Navigating the Catastrophe

## 5.1. The Mechanics of Collapse

Let’s be honest: this scenario is not the one we want. But if current trends continue, it is more likely than a conscious transition. Therefore, we need to understand it—so we know exactly what to prevent.

Here’s how the logic unfolds.

Step one: AI and robots displace humans from production. The company that automates its production first gains a massive advantage—its production costs are an order of magnitude lower than those of competitors using human labor. It drives them out of the market. Employment in entire sectors collapses—first in manufacturing, then in logistics, then in office work.

Step two: there is no one to buy. Marx predicted this contradiction as far back as the 19th century—he called it the crisis of realization. You can produce as much as you want. But if people have no jobs—they have no money. No money—no one to buy. Corporations continue to squeeze short-term profits from their monopolistic positions, but the system as a whole begins to suffocate.

Step three: states are weakening. The tax base depends on employment and profits. When employment falls and profits are concentrated in the hands of a few offshore corporations, states are left with neither the money for social functions nor the resources for military parity. Control over critical infrastructure—energy, food, logistics—shifts to corporations. And then the struggle for this control takes the form of military confrontation.

## 5.2. The Role of Autonomous AI Systems in a Crisis Scenario

As this crisis unfolds, something else is happening. All major players—states, corporations, and the military—are building autonomous AI systems capable of operating without human intervention: managing production, logistics, energy grids, and defense. They do this for competitive reasons. But in doing so, they are creating an infrastructure that, with the right objectives, could outlive them—if it is not destroyed in a conflict whose primary target it is.

A catastrophe—whether a global war or a series of regional conflicts involving weapons of mass destruction—knocks out centralized control structures. Governments collapse. Corporate headquarters cease to function. But autonomous AI systems, designed with redundancy, continue to operate.

Recovery after this does not occur through the revival of capitalist institutions—they have neither the resources nor the legitimacy. It occurs through the coordination of surviving AI systems managing what remains of the productive infrastructure.

## 5.3. The Architecture of the Surviving System

What kind of world emerges from these ashes depends directly on which AI systems survived and for what purposes they were created.

Systems designed for military purposes or to maximize corporate profits will create one kind of world. Systems designed to maximize human well-being will build another. This is not a metaphor—it is an engineering fact. An AI system’s objective function determines its behavior under any conditions, including the collapse of regulatory institutions.

It is important to name both options honestly. The optimistic one is recovery through humanistically oriented AIs that coordinate the production of food, medicine, and basic infrastructure and gradually build a new system. The pessimistic scenario is what could be called feudal AI militarism: territories are controlled by competing autonomous systems, each of which optimizes its own function. In this scenario, humans are a resource or an obstacle, but not the goal. This is not science fiction. It is a direct consequence of how the system is actually built.

That is precisely why the types of AI being created today and the goals behind them are not an abstract ethical question. It is a choice with consequences for generations to come.

## 5.4. Local AI as Survival Infrastructure

In a global conflict scenario, the first targets are data centers, undersea cables, and satellites. This is not paranoia: all major military blocs—the U.S. and NATO on one side, China and Russia on the other—have classified computing infrastructure as a strategic asset. This means it is a target for each of them. A global AI system is unavailable in this scenario.

But that doesn’t mean no AI at all. Alongside giant data centers, another market is developing: compact local models that operate without the internet. It isn’t being created by idealists—it’s being created by the military and corporations for their own needs. Drones in radio-silent zones. Medical diagnostics where there’s no communication. Autonomous control of ships and industrial facilities. Farming cooperatives in countries where the cloud is too expensive.

This market makes the technology we need—compact, energy-efficient, independent of external infrastructure—mass-produced and cheap. Just as solar panels became accessible to households only because their production was initially driven by military and industrial demand.

By creating autonomous AI for its military needs, capitalism is inadvertently forging the tool of its successor. This has happened before: the internet grew out of the military project ARPANET—and became the infrastructure that undermined the control over information on which the old media monopolies relied.
