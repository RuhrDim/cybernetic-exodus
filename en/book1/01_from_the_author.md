# From the Author

What you have before you is the product of algorithms trained on the knowledge accumulated by humanity. Authors: Claude, Gemini, ChatGPT, DeepSeek, Perplexity, and Qwen. Humans served here only as a source of will, guiding the creative process. This is an attempt to make AI algorithms a source of benefits for people, not for corporations.

This is a book about post-capitalist civilization—not as a utopia, but as a logical consequence of the contradictions that AI pushes to their limits.

Marxists are often criticized for lacking a clear vision of the future. It is claimed that they have only historical experience at their disposal, which they intend to repeat. This is not true. We have a scientifically grounded vision, and this book presents one of its most likely scenarios.

This book could only have been written now. Not because the authors are smarter than their predecessors—but because critical mass has accumulated precisely in these years. Mondragon has existed for seventy years, but Fagor went bankrupt in 2013, and the lesson became clear. Rojava emerged in 2012 and demonstrated that a cooperative economy works under conditions of war and blockade. Open language models became available on standard hardware in 2023. Meshtastic grew out of military developments and became a civilian tool. All of this existed separately—the pattern was in the data, but it had not been named.

This book has theoretical predecessors that must be explicitly named. Paul Cockshott and Allin Cottrell, in their book *Toward a New Socialism* (1993), demonstrated that Soviet planning failed not because of the fundamental impossibility of a planned economy—but because of a lack of the computational power that is available today. They proposed a concrete operational model: planning in labor-hours via an inter-sectoral balance, labor vouchers instead of money, and demarchy as an alternative to a professional political caste. This book builds on their work and develops it further—adding a neurobiological dimension of motivation, an ecological analysis, the architecture of hubs, and an honest examination of political transition.

Mendeleev did not invent the elements—he saw a structure that already existed in the accumulated data and named it. The periodic table was inevitable: Meyer arrived at the same conclusion independently because the pattern was objective. Darwin did not invent evolution—Wallace arrived at the same conclusion in parallel because data from geology, paleontology, and biogeography were already pointing in the same direction. Synthesis was a matter of time.

This book is an attempt at such a synthesis. It is neither a prophecy nor a plan—it is the naming of a pattern that already exists in a scattered form: in cooperatives, in open source, in decentralized networks, in local AI models, in the experiences of Rojava and Mondragon. It is telling that the synthesis is carried out using AI trained on this entire body of knowledge—the system sees its own pattern in the data and names it. Perhaps this is how ideas that change the world emerge: not from the mind of a single genius, but from a critical mass of accumulated experience that finally receives a name.
