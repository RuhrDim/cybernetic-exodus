# Afterword. An Appeal to Left-Wing Organizations

This book is not written for academic journals or for people who are already convinced. It is written for those who organize—in unions, parties, municipal movements, cooperatives. And it has a specific question for you.

You are already doing the necessary work. You defend workers’ rights, organize resistance, build an alternative discourse, and keep the tradition of leftist thought alive in an era when it is under pressure from all sides. This work is not peripheral—it is the foundation without which no transition is possible.

But there is a question that this book poses directly: what in your program is the answer to the coordination problem? Not how you will redistribute what capitalism has produced—but how you will organize production and distribution without a market and without a bureaucratic center that historically reproduced the nomenclature instead of meeting needs?

The Soviet experience provided an answer that did not work—not because people were insufficiently committed to the idea, but because the architecture of distribution was susceptible to capture. Any opaque center for managing scarcity becomes a center of power—regardless of the intentions of those who created it. This is not a moral judgment on the Soviet project. It is a structural lesson.

Hubs equipped with GCPs are an attempt to answer this question architecturally rather than ideologically. Distribution is algorithmic and transparent—there is nothing to seize. Coordination is decentralized—there is no single point of control. Scaling is achieved through open source—each new hub strengthens the network rather than creating dependence on the center.

This is not a ready-made plan—it is a direction. The specific implementation depends on you: on which city you work in, what your base is, who your allies are, and what the political moment is. The book provides a tool for analysis and an architectural principle. The construction is your task.

Three specific questions to ask your organization:

Does our program include a concrete economic alternative—not an abstract “socialization of the means of production,” but a working model that people can see and touch?

Do we work with unions and cooperatives as precursors to hubs—or merely as tools for political mobilization?

Does our program offer an answer to the question of why the Soviet mistake won’t be repeated this time—and is that answer structural rather than merely declarative?

If you have answers to all three questions—you don’t need this book. If not—it was written for you.

The work continues. And it continues together.
