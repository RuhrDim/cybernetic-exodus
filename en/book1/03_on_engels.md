# On the attitude toward Engels and “The Principles of Communism”

In 1847, Friedrich Engels wrote “Principles of Communism”—a text in the form of questions and answers that became the immediate precursor to the “Manifesto.” There, he formulated the logic of the transition from capitalism to communism as precisely and consistently as the knowledge and historical experience of his era allowed. This book exists in dialogue with that text—but not in dispute with it.

We do not claim to be “correcting” or “rewriting” Engels. That would be presumptuous and fundamentally incorrect. Engels gave us not a set of answers, but a method for posing questions: how the productive forces of a given era are structured, what relations of production correspond to them, where contradictions accumulate between them, and who is the agent of their resolution. It is precisely this method that we apply—to conditions that did not exist in 1847 and could not have existed.

Engels could not have considered artificial intelligence as a productive force—not because he lacked insight, but because the phenomenon did not exist. He could not have drawn on the neuroscience of motivation, the theory of complex systems, or computational planning theory. By applying his method to these new conditions, we are doing what he himself would likely have done in our place.

Where our analysis diverges from Engels’s—and why this is not a contradiction

In “Principles,” the revolutionary subject is the proletariat: a class deprived of the means of production, with a material interest in changing the system and a numerical majority to bring it about. We do not reject this thesis. We complicate it.

The automation of production creates a situation that Engels could not have foreseen: the traditional industrial proletariat is shrinking, but at the same time the circle of those whom the system exploits or excludes is expanding—employed industrial and service workers, precarious workers, cognitive labor deprived of a share in the value created, and the unemployed displaced by automation. All of them are deprived of the means of production and have a material interest in changing the system—regardless of whether they are inside or outside the labor market. Ultimately, this circle encompasses the majority of the planet’s population. The material interest in changing the system does not disappear—it is merely distributed differently.

But this majority still lacks the tool to solve the coordination problem: how to organize production and distribution without a market and without a bureaucratic center. This is precisely where AI comes in—not as a replacement for the subject, but as a tool that has historically been missing. The proletariat (in the broad sense—everyone whom the system pushes aside) provides the political will and legitimacy for the transition. AI provides the computational tool for its implementation. Neither one nor the other can make the transition alone: AI without the support of the majority has neither agency nor the right to act; the majority without AI runs up against the same wall of coordination that has thwarted all previous attempts.

This is a dialectical pair, not an alternative.

What this book adds beyond Engels

In “Principles,” Engels describes the transitional period in detail: the concrete measures that the victorious proletariat implements through the state on the path from capitalism to communism. This book explores the transitional period in much greater detail than was possible in the classical tradition—through an analysis of the precursor chain of hubs (6.10), the role of trade unions and parties as organizing forces (6.10), strategies in conditions of crisis and fascistization (6.8), the economic fronts of confrontation with the capitalist system (6.9), the international dimension and defense through the global nature of the movement (6.11), and the evolutionary path of existing cooperative structures through local AI (6.12).

What remains open—and is honestly acknowledged—is:

First—a concrete political theory of seizing power in the 21st century. The book describes the agents of transition and the strategy for accumulating counter-hegemony. But the question of exactly how the final political transition occurs—through institutional reforms, through the growing functional failure of old institutions, or through the “crystallization strategy” described in Open Questions—remains a subject for further theoretical work.

Second—the detailed operational architecture of the GCP. The book describes the principles and objective function. Specific algorithms, consensus protocols, and verification mechanisms require separate technical development—this is an engineering task, not a theoretical one.

Third—the theory of the revolutionary subject as applied to specific national contexts. The class structure in Germany, India, and Brazil differs so greatly that a single answer is impossible. The book provides an analytical tool—its application to specific conditions remains the task of local movements.

The authors clearly mark this boundary because honesty regarding the limits of their own analysis is part of the very method they follow.
