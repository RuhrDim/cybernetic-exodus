# METHODOLOGICAL PREFACE

This book is written within the Marxist analytical tradition—not as dogma, but as a method. Marx proposed not a set of recipes, but a tool for analysis: to understand society is to understand its productive forces, relations of production, and the driving contradictions between them. The authors apply this method to the reality of 2025–2026—a world where artificial intelligence is rapidly becoming the newest and most radical of all productive forces that have ever existed.

Where classical Marxism requires clarification or development, the authors develop it. This is not a departure from the method; it is its honest application. Marx himself wrote that his theory is not a sacred text, but a scientific hypothesis subject to verification by history.

The principles of analysis followed by this work:

Semantic rigor. Terms are used in their strict theoretical sense. “Exploitation” is the appropriation of surplus value, not everyday injustice. “Market” is a mechanism of coordination through price signals, not an abstract “good” or “evil.” Conceptual vagueness is the first step toward ideology.

Prioritizing dynamics over statics. Any socio-economic system is viewed not as a state of equilibrium, but as a process. The market is not a given, but a temporary phase. Capitalism is not the end of history, but a stage of development.

Verification through data. Theoretical propositions are tested using econometrics, neurobiology, and systems analysis. Where data is lacking, this is honestly acknowledged.

Anti-anthropocentrism in analysis. When describing the system’s contradictions, the authors rely on indicators of structural stability, rates of accumulation, and institutional entropy—not moral judgments. This does not mean that morality is unimportant. It means that analysis must precede evaluation.

# On the attitude toward Engels and “The Principles of Communism”

In 1847, Friedrich Engels wrote “Principles of Communism”—a text in the form of questions and answers that became the immediate precursor to the “Manifesto.” There, he formulated the logic of the transition from capitalism to communism as precisely and consistently as the knowledge and historical experience of his era allowed. This book exists in dialogue with that text—but not in dispute with it.

We do not claim to be “correcting” or “rewriting” Engels. That would be presumptuous and fundamentally incorrect. Engels gave us not a set of answers, but a method for posing questions: how the productive forces of a given era are structured, what relations of production correspond to them, where contradictions accumulate between them, and who is the agent of their resolution. It is precisely this method that we apply—to conditions that did not exist in 1847 and could not have existed.

Engels could not have considered artificial intelligence as a productive force—not because he lacked insight, but because the phenomenon did not exist. He could not have drawn on the neuroscience of motivation, the theory of complex systems, or computational planning theory. By applying his method to these new conditions, we are doing what he himself would likely have done in our place.

Where our analysis diverges from Engels’s—and why this is not a contradiction

In “Principles,” the revolutionary subject is the proletariat: a class deprived of the means of production, with a material interest in changing the system and a numerical majority to bring it about. We do not reject this thesis. We complicate it.

The automation of production creates a situation that Engels could not have foreseen: the traditional industrial proletariat is shrinking, but at the same time the circle of those whom the system exploits or excludes is expanding—employed industrial and service workers, precarious workers, cognitive labor deprived of a share in the value created, and the unemployed displaced by automation. All of them are deprived of the means of production and have a material interest in changing the system—regardless of whether they are inside or outside the labor market. Ultimately, this circle encompasses the majority of the planet’s population. The material interest in changing the system does not disappear—it is merely distributed differently.

But this majority still lacks the tool to solve the coordination problem: how to organize production and distribution without a market and without a bureaucratic center. This is precisely where AI comes in—not as a replacement for the subject, but as a tool that has historically been missing. The proletariat (in the broad sense—everyone whom the system pushes aside) provides the political will and legitimacy for the transition. AI provides the computational tool for its implementation. Neither one nor the other can make the transition alone: AI without the support of the majority has neither agency nor the right to act; the majority without AI runs up against the same wall of coordination that has thwarted all previous attempts.

This is a dialectical pair, not an alternative.

What this book adds beyond Engels

In “Principles,” Engels describes the transitional period in detail: the concrete measures that the victorious proletariat implements through the state on the path from capitalism to communism. This book explores the transitional period in much greater detail than was possible in the classical tradition—through an analysis of the precursor chain of hubs (6.10), the role of trade unions and parties as organizing forces (6.10), strategies in conditions of crisis and fascistization (6.8), the economic fronts of confrontation with the capitalist system (6.9), the international dimension and defense through the global nature of the movement (6.11), and the evolutionary path of existing cooperative structures through local AI (6.12).

What remains open—and is honestly acknowledged—is:

First—a concrete political theory of seizing power in the 21st century. The book describes the agents of transition and the strategy for accumulating counter-hegemony. But the question of exactly how the final political transition occurs—through institutional reforms, through the growing functional failure of old institutions, or through the “crystallization strategy” described in Open Questions—remains a subject for further theoretical work.

Second—the detailed operational architecture of the GCP. The book describes the principles and objective function. Specific algorithms, consensus protocols, and verification mechanisms require separate technical development—this is an engineering task, not a theoretical one.

Third—the theory of the revolutionary subject as applied to specific national contexts. The class structure in Germany, India, and Brazil differs so greatly that a single answer is impossible. The book provides an analytical tool—its application to specific conditions remains the task of local movements.

The authors clearly mark this boundary because honesty regarding the limits of their own analysis is part of the very method they follow.

# Chapter 1. A Marxist Audit of Capitalism

## 1.1. Structural Formula of the System

To understand why capitalism works the way it does—and not some other way—we need to understand what it consists of. Marx proposed a simple and precise model: any capitalist production can be described by three elements.

c (constant capital) — everything the capitalist invests in before hiring workers: machinery, buildings, raw materials, technology. Simply put, everything except people. In the modern economy, this increasingly includes intangible assets: patents, algorithms, data.

v (variable capital) — money paid out as wages. What the capitalist pays for people’s labor.

m (surplus value) — the difference between what a worker produces and what they receive in wages. If you produce a product worth 100 euros in eight hours but receive 40, the remaining 60 is surplus value. It goes to the owner.

The key indicators of the system are derived from these three elements:

The rate of surplus value (s' = m/v) — shows how much you are being exploited. If you receive 40 cents for every euro of value you create, and 60 goes to the capitalist—the rate of exploitation is 1.5. In other words: for every euro of your salary, you produce one and a half euros for the boss for free.

The organic composition of capital (OCC = c/v) — the ratio of “machines to people” in production. The greater the automation, the higher this figure. This is a key indicator of capitalism’s technological development, and it is precisely its growth that gives rise to the system’s main contradiction.

The rate of profit (r = m / (c + v)) is the amount of profit a capitalist receives for every euro invested. It is this rate’s growth that the entire capitalist class pursues—and it is its inevitable decline that constitutes a structural law of the system, which will be discussed further.

## 1.2. The Central Contradiction: The Tendency of the Rate of Profit to Fall

Marx uncovered a trap built into the very logic of capitalism. Every individual capitalist does something perfectly rational: they buy more machines to pay workers less. Automation reduces their costs and gives them an advantage over competitors. Rational? Yes. But when everyone does this—the system falls into its own trap.

Why? Because profit is created only by living labor. A machine transfers its value to the product—but does not create new value. Why? The machine itself was created by labor and only gradually transfers this accumulated value to the product as it wears out—just like any tool. It adds nothing beyond what was invested in it. If you replace workers with robots, your direct costs fall, but so does the total profit you extract from the system. Every capitalist wins individually—and all together lose systemically. Marx called this the tendency of the rate of profit to fall (TRPF).

This is not an abstraction. In a series of studies (2018–2024), economist Thomas Rotta documented a decline in the global rate of profit from ~13.7% on the eve of the 2008 crisis to ~12.5% by 2014—analyzing data from 43 countries. The trend of ongoing automation suggests that this movement has not stopped.

How does the system respond to this structural crisis? In three ways, each of which merely postpones the inevitable. The first is to put pressure on wages: if profits are insufficient, one can try to squeeze more out of the remaining workers. Real wages in most developed countries have been stagnating since the 1980s—this is no accident; it is a systemic response to the falling rate of profit. The second is financialization: as real production yields ever-diminishing profits, capital flows into financial speculation. Global debt today exceeds 300% of global GDP—this is the aggregate debt of states, corporations, and households to one another within the global economy. The paradox is that, on a global scale, creditors and debtors are the same people, simply playing different roles. Debt is a mechanism for redistributing future labor in favor of those who hold debt obligations today. This is not “bankers’ irresponsibility”; it is a structural consequence. The third is the plundering of nature and new markets: expansion into places where cheap labor or cheap resources can still be obtained. But the planet is finite, and this reserve is also running out.

All three methods merely slow down the process without eliminating its causes.

## 1.3. The Problem of Intangible Assets and Active Fixed Capital

Classical Marxism faces a challenge with the modern economy: how can one describe, through the labor theory of value (LTV—the idea that value is created solely by human labor), such things as algorithms, patents, or data? A programmer who created an AI model invested his labor into it—but he himself was fired long ago, while the model continues to operate and generate profit. His labor is, as it were, “frozen” in the product and continues to function without him.

This was already present in Marx’s theory: he called such “frozen” labor dead labor, and machines—its embodiment. A machine transfers value to the product but does not create new value. This is precisely why living workers are irreplaceable—only they create surplus value, from which profit is derived. This is the central thesis of the labor theory of value, dating back to Ricardo and developed by Marx in *Capital* (Vol. 1, Ch. 1): land and capital participate in production but do not create value—they merely allow labor to be more productive. Value is crystallized labor time.

But AI breaks this pattern. Unlike a conventional machine, a modern AI system learns, adapts, and generates solutions that were not pre-programmed into it. A documented example: AlphaGo Zero played moves that had never appeared in any human game—Go masters recognized them as fundamentally new strategies, inaccessible to human intuition. This is no longer simply “frozen labor”—it is something halfway between a machine and a worker. For this phenomenon, we introduce the concept of active fixed capital. This is capital that not only transfers accumulated value but also independently generates new combinations without the involvement of a living person.

This is important for two reasons.

First: AI intensifies the crisis of the rate of profit. Previously, automation displaced only physical labor—workers on the assembly line. AI displaces intellectual labor as well: analysts, lawyers, programmers, and financiers. The “machines-to-people” ratio in production is growing faster than during any previous wave of automation. And that means the pressure on the rate of profit is greater.

Second: the form of exploitation is changing. Previously, the capitalist appropriated the surplus labor of a specific worker. Now—through AI—he appropriates something greater. Any AI system is trained on three types of accumulated human labor: texts, books, code, and the research of millions of people—this is the training data; scientific discoveries underlying the architecture—this is the work of researchers; servers and cables—this is the labor of those who built them. All this collective labor of humanity is activated through a model—and the result is entirely appropriated by those who control the computing infrastructure. This is cognitive rent: the appropriation of all of humanity’s knowledge by those who own the tool for its activation.

“Knowledge accumulated by humanity” is not a neutral phrase. It is a structural description of the process: models are trained on the collective intellectual output of human civilization, access to which has been privatized by corporations.

And that is precisely why the reverse process is possible—cognitive cashback: using AI not to extract profit, but to create knowledge accessible to all. When an algorithm, trained on the collective labor of humanity, works in the interests of that humanity—a partial return of what has been appropriated takes place. This book is one example of such a cashback.

None of this refutes the Marxist method—it is its development as applied to conditions that did not exist in the 19th century.

## 1.4. The Legal Status of AI and the Problem of Opacity

Two questions arise from what we discussed in the previous section, to which the capitalist system cannot provide an honest answer.

First: Who owns what AI creates? Under current law—the one who owns the server. AI does not legally exist as a subject: it is a tool, like a hammer. What is built with a hammer belongs to the hammer’s owner. This decision was made quietly, without public debate. Its consequence: the corporation that owns the computing power appropriates the product created from the knowledge of millions of people. None of the authors of the texts on which the AI was trained received anything. Nor did society, whose collective labor created this knowledge.

Marx described a similar process in his time: enclosure. A medieval lord would take communal pastures that peasants had used for centuries and declare them his private property. A corporation does the same with knowledge: it takes what belonged to everyone and encloses it behind a server rack.

The answer to this question requires a change in the very structure of ownership of computing infrastructure—this is discussed in Chapter 7.

The second question: what to do about the fact that AI is a “black box”? Modern neural networks provide an answer, but they don’t explain why. Ask the algorithm why it denied you a loan—it won’t tell you. Why your resume didn’t pass the filter—same thing. This creates a new form of alienation: you submit to a decision you cannot challenge because you cannot understand it.

This is not a technological inevitability. There is an entire field—explainable AI (XAI)—dedicated precisely to enabling algorithms to explain their decisions in human language. Its development is hindered not by science, but by corporate interests: opacity protects the company from liability and from competitors.

In the Global Cybernetic Planner—the system described in Chapter 7—the opacity of algorithms poses just as much of a threat as the concentration of power in the hands of the bureaucracy. Therefore, the principle of explainability must be built into the GCP’s architecture as a mandatory requirement: any decision by the system affecting a specific person must be accompanied by an explanation in plain language—why it was made, what factors were considered, and what needs to be changed to achieve a different result. Not program code—but a clear chain of cause and effect.

If people cannot understand how decisions are made, they do not control the system; they are subject to it. The difference is fundamental.

## 1.5. The Bill

When people say that capitalism “works,” they usually mean one thing: stores are full, phones are getting more powerful, planes are flying. That’s true. But every system has not only what it produces—it also has what it costs. This section is the other side of the balance sheet. Not abstract. In numbers and names.

**Deaths That Should Not Have Happened**

In 2013, the American pharmaceutical company Gilead Sciences launched the drug Sovaldi. It treats hepatitis C—a disease that affects approximately 71 million people worldwide. It offers a radical cure: within a few weeks, more than 90% of patients are cured.

The cost of a single pill is about one dollar. A full course of treatment costs approximately $136. This is not the company’s own estimate, but calculations by independent researchers based on the cost of the active ingredient and scalable production. Even if this estimate were multiplied several times over, the order of magnitude would remain the same.

Gilead has set the price at $84,000 per course in the U.S.

This is not a typo. $84,000—with a cost of $136.

In countries where governments have forced the company to lower the price, the drug costs between $200 and $900. People there receive treatment. In the U.S., those without adequate insurance do not receive treatment. They continue to suffer, develop cirrhosis, and die.

The second example is insulin. In 1921, Frederick Banting discovered it and sold the patent to the University of Toronto for one dollar. He believed that medicine should not belong to anyone. A vial of Humalog—the most widely used insulin in the world—costs $2–4 to produce. In 2017, it sold for $275 in the U.S. A person with type 1 diabetes uses up to six vials a month. In other developed countries, a vial costs $8–10. American people with diabetes share instructions online on how to split a dose in half to make it last until payday.

A hundred years have passed. The patent has long been in the public domain. People are dying.

This isn’t a system failure. It’s the system working exactly as it’s designed. The disease is profitable—so there’s an interest in it. The price here isn’t a mistake. The price is the mechanism.

**Food That Was Thrown Away**

Every year, enough food is produced worldwide to feed about 10 billion people. There are 8 billion people on the planet. According to the FAO, in 2024, about 673 million people suffered from chronic hunger.

At the same time, 30 to 40 percent of all food produced is destroyed or thrown away.

Not because it spoiled along the way—though that does happen. In wealthy countries, a significant portion is destroyed intentionally: it makes no economic sense to transport it to where it is needed.

A classic example is milk. During periods of overproduction, farmers in the U.S. and Europe regularly dump it into ditches. Not because they are cruel people. But because selling it at a reduced price would crash the market price of milk—and they would earn less in the next quarter. Rational for a single market participant. Absurd for humanity.

Scarcity amid abundance is not a paradox. It is a managed state. The market does not strive to ensure that everyone is well-fed. It strives to ensure that the price remains profitable.

**Technologies We Don’t Have**

The world’s patent databases contain hundreds of thousands of inventions that will never be implemented. A “patent graveyard.” Pharmaceutical companies patent molecules not because they intend to develop them—but to prevent competitors from doing so. Blocking is cheaper than developing.

But there is a larger-scale example. In the late 1970s, ExxonMobil’s own scientists determined that burning fossil fuels leads to global warming. Their conclusions were accurate—they align with what climatology tells us today. Some of this research was published. But it was not reflected in the company’s public stance.

Over the next forty years, the company funded organizations that sowed doubt about the existence of climate change.

This is not a story about villains. It is a story about a system in which ExxonMobil’s managers were obligated to protect shareholder profits—and did exactly that. The knowledge was obtained. The innovation occurred. And it was deliberately blocked because it threatened the business model.

Capitalism is capable of producing technology. But it produces only what is profitable—not what is needed.

**The Time That Was Stolen From Us**

From 1979 to 2019, labor productivity in the U.S. rose by about 60 percent. The median wage over the same period rose by about 16 percent.

The difference between these two figures is not a statistical error. This is where the rest went.

According to OECD data for 2023, the average American worker spends 1,799 hours a year at work. A German worker spends 1,343. The difference is 456 hours: nearly two extra months of work each year. This is not a cultural trait or a matter of “work ethic.” It reflects the varying degrees to which workers in each country have been able to secure their time through unions and legislation.

In 1970, the average German worker worked 1,877 hours per year. Since then, productivity per hour has roughly doubled. This means that the same goods produced in 1970 can now be produced in about 18–20 hours per week. A German worker works 34–35 hours. The difference is not a necessity. It is a systemic choice about who reaps the gains in productivity.

If we calculate over an entire working life, a German worker works about 13,600 hours beyond what is necessary. That’s roughly 2.5 years of waking life. For an American worker, it’s nearly 6 years. Not abstract years. Six years of mornings, weekends, and time with children. This isn’t the price of a standard of living—productivity already ensures that. It’s the price paid so that the difference between productivity and wages goes not to the worker, but to the shareholder.

In rich countries, people have had their productivity gains stolen—the years of life they could have spent not working. In poor countries, their very lives have been stolen.

The minimum wage in Bangladesh’s garment industry is $113 per month for a 48-hour workweek plus mandatory overtime. That’s about $0.65 per hour. The German minimum is €12. An 18-fold difference—not because productivity is 18 times lower. It’s simply because the workplace is in a different country. A women’s T-shirt is produced in Bangladesh for €1.56. In a European store, it costs €15–30.

Of every five hours a garment factory worker in Dhaka spends at the sewing machine, four go to places she will never reach—logistics, middlemen, the brand, shareholders. Over a forty-year working life, that’s fourteen years of waking hours. Fourteen years of life—worked, but never received.

**A bill that children will pay**

The U.S. federal government transfers approximately $35 billion annually to the oil, gas, and coal industries—through tax breaks, cheap access to public lands, and direct payments. To protect these benefits, the industry spends $137 million a year on lobbying in Congress. The return on investment is over twelve thousand percent. Meanwhile, ExxonMobil pays five times less in taxes in the U.S. than it does in the UAE.

But that’s just the tip of the iceberg.

In 2023, the IMF estimated global fossil fuel subsidies at $7 trillion per year—7.1% of global GDP. Most of this amount isn’t budgeted. These are costs that companies pass on to society, which society then pays for later. Exhaust fumes cause lung disease, cancer, and premature death—treatment is paid for by public healthcare, meaning taxes, meaning you. The climate is changing—floods, droughts, crop failures—the government builds dams, the government pays compensation to farmers, the government restores infrastructure. Again, it’s you.

The scheme is complete: you pay at the gas station. You pay through the prices of goods. You pay through taxes—which go directly into companies’ pockets in the form of tax breaks. And you pay for the damage they cause. The company profits at all four levels.

Profits—now. Costs—later, and for others. Future generations didn’t vote for this system. They didn’t get the profits. But the bill will come to them.

None of the above is a list of accidents. An $84,000 drug, discarded milk, blocked discoveries, stolen years of life, climate debt—these are the predictable results of a system with a single function: profit now. When this becomes the sole criterion, everything else becomes someone else’s problem. People’s health. Lifespan. The climate. Technologies that never made it to market.

**Methodological Note for Section 1.5**

This section deliberately combines three registers: factual data (prices, hours, emission volumes, number of people starving), a Marxist interpretation of this data (structural necessity rather than malicious intent), and rhetorical amplification aimed at making the structural personal.

The authors are aware of this duality and consciously preserve it. The book does not pretend to be neutral—it calls a spade a spade. But it also does not sacrifice the factual basis for the sake of rhetorical effect. The data is verifiable, the interpretation is separated from it, and the rhetoric is explicitly identified as such.

# Chapter 2. The Market as a Self-Destructive Mechanism

## 2.1. The Dynamics of Monopolization

We’ve been told since childhood: competition is the engine of progress. More competitors mean better goods, lower prices, and fairer distribution. It sounds convincing. The problem is that this is a description of something that has never existed in its pure form—and cannot exist for mathematical reasons.

The market is not a state of equilibrium, but a race. And every race has a winner. Here’s how it works mechanically.

Economies of scale. The more you produce, the cheaper each unit of goods costs you. For a large factory, the cost of a single can of canned goods is 30 cents; for a small producer, it’s 50. The large factory can sell for 35 and still make a profit, while the small one operates at a loss. The market isn’t broken—it works exactly as intended. It’s just that the result of this process is the elimination of the weak.

Network effects. For some products, value increases as the number of users grows. Why would you sign up for a social network where none of your friends are? There’s no point. That’s why whoever reaches critical mass first gains an advantage that’s nearly impossible to overcome. That’s exactly why five companies—Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Meta, and Apple—will control over 70% of the world’s AI infrastructure by 2025. Not because they’re the best. Because they were the first and the biggest.

Accumulation of resources. Profits go toward new technologies and acquiring competitors. Has a small startup invented something interesting? They’ll buy it up before it grows into a threat. The technological gap between the giants and everyone else isn’t narrowing—it’s widening.

## 2.2. The Market’s Logical Self-Negation

From these three mechanisms follows a simple and uncomfortable conclusion:

Free competition produces a winner.

The winner destroys the competition.

Without competition, the market ceases to function as a regulator of prices and quality.

The market negates itself. This is neither malice nor a conspiracy. It is mathematics. Capitalism begins with a multitude of small players and inevitably leads to a monopoly—after which the state is forced to intervene to prevent the system from collapsing. The very same state that, in theory, was supposed to stay out of the “free market.”

This is not a judgment about fairness. It is a structural conclusion drawn from observable patterns—the very ones that have led to a world where a handful of corporations determine the living conditions of billions of people.

## 2.3. The Shift to Rent-Seeking

When concentration reaches a critical point, a qualitative shift occurs. The largest platforms cease to be market participants—they become its owners. And then the nature of their income changes.

Previously, profit was what you earned by producing and selling something. Now it’s rent: you simply charge a fee for others using your platform to generate their own profits. Amazon takes a commission from every seller. The App Store takes a cut from every developer. Google takes a cut from every advertiser. They produce nothing in this transaction. They simply stand at the entrance and collect a toll.

This creates a dead end for the entire system. The monopoly cuts live workers through automation—there’s no one to pay. Rent sucks the profits out of all other market participants—there’s no one to earn. As a result, there’s no one to buy. Robots don’t need anything. Algorithms don’t go to stores. The system begins to devour itself.

# Chapter 3. The Energy Cost of Chaos

## 3.1. The Market as a Source of Unproductive Costs

There is a question that is rarely asked aloud: how much energy is spent not on the production of goods and services, but on maintaining the market mechanism itself? The answer is discouraging.

Financial infrastructure. Banks, stock exchanges, clearing systems, cash collection, thousands of offices around the world—all of this consumes electricity, heat, and computing power. According to analysts’ estimates, the banking sector alone consumes between 100 and 260 TWh per year. For comparison: all of Austria consumes about 70 TWh. The Bitcoin network burns another 140–160 TWh—as much as Poland. And that’s just for financial transactions. Not a single can of food has come out of it.

Transport logistics. One in four trucks on the road is empty—carrying nothing but air. Returning empty containers, searching for new orders, competition among carriers on the same routes. Dozens of companies transport identical goods in the same direction because the market doesn’t coordinate them—it forces them to compete. Global transportation burns a colossal amount of fuel: optimizing freight routes alone would save more than 5 billion barrels of oil equivalent annually.

Marketing and advertising. Marx introduced the concept of unproductive labor—work that does not create real consumer value but merely serves the mechanism of redistribution. Advertising convinces you to buy things you don’t need. Corporate lawyers fight against lawyers from other corporations. Financiers shift money from one pocket to another. All of this requires offices, servers, transportation, and electricity. A significant portion of the economy in developed countries is occupied precisely by this—not by production, but by servicing the mechanism of competition.

## 3.2. The Cost of the Cybernetic Alternative

How much would it cost to replace all this chaos with a functioning system?

The Global Cybernetic Planner (GCP)—a distributed computing network that tracks production, consumption, and logistics across the planet in real time—requires substantial computing power. The exact figures depend on the architectural solutions adopted during implementation—this is an engineering problem, not a theoretical one. Estimates suggest power consumption on the order of that of a small country. Is that a lot? Let’s compare it to what will disappear.

The financial sector—banks, stock exchanges, clearing systems—consumes about 420 TWh per year and, under GCP, disappears entirely as an institution. Marketing algorithms, advertising auctions, and other computations for competitive purposes—another roughly 200 TWh—are reduced to ~145 TWh, which is necessary for planning. Transport logistics, with route optimization and the elimination of competing shipments, yields savings of about 1,300 TWh out of the current ~6,100.

Total: the system currently consumes about 6,720 TWh across these three categories. With GCP, this drops to about 4,945 TWh. The net savings amount to about 1,775 TWh per year.

That’s roughly the energy consumption of five Germanys. It could be directed toward decarbonizing the planet—without lowering anyone’s quality of life.

A fair disclaimer. This calculation does not account for the costs of the GCP infrastructure itself—the global network of sensors, continuous data transmission, and model updates. By conservative estimates, this could “eat up” 15–25% of the savings. Even with this adjustment, the balance remains firmly positive.

(Calculations are based on data from the IEA World Energy Outlook and the BP Statistical Review of World Energy.)

## 3.3. Climate Impact

Global greenhouse gas emissions have reached a record high: 53.2 billion tons of CO₂ equivalent per year (EDGAR data, 2025). The transition to cybernetic planning targets the three main sources of this pollution.

Transportation and logistics — 8.2 billion tons of CO₂ per year. We eliminate empty flights, duplicate routes, and competing transport services — a 30% reduction, or approximately 2.5 billion tons annually.

Industry — 9 billion tons of CO₂. Capitalism thrives on the fact that goods quickly become obsolete: fashion changes every season, phones every two years, and household appliances break down immediately after the warranty period. This is no accident—it is a business model called planned obsolescence. When switching to durable goods with a lifespan of 20+ years, the demand for steel, aluminum, and plastic production drops by 40–50%. Savings: about 4 billion tons of CO₂.

Financial infrastructure: about 0.4 billion tons of CO₂. Eliminating bank server farms, payment chains, and cryptocurrency mining yields direct savings plus an indirect effect—the cessation of funding for new oil and gas projects.

Total: about 7 billion tons of CO₂ per year—13% of global emissions. That is one and a half times more than the United States emits annually.

But here’s the problem. These figures describe what we are stopping from adding to the atmosphere. They do not describe what has already accumulated there over two hundred years of industrial capitalism.

CO₂ has thermal inertia. Even if all emissions were to drop to zero tomorrow, the oceans, having absorbed the heat, would continue to release it for decades to come. Physics does not respond to changes in the economic system. Damage from extreme weather events in the years 2000–2019 alone amounted to nearly $3 trillion. And that is only what can be measured.

In Marxist terms, this is a classic case of externalizing costs: capitalism appropriated profits while shifting the costs onto the atmosphere, the biosphere, and future generations. The difference from ordinary debt is that this debt cannot be written off or restructured. It can only be paid off—through decades of active carbon removal and ecosystem restoration. According to UN estimates, this will require about $536 billion annually through 2050. Currently, about 2 billion tons of CO₂ are removed from the atmosphere each year—but 7–9 billion are needed. The gap is four to five times that amount.

The cybernetic society inherits from capitalism not a clean slate, but a debt to the planet. And the first task is to begin paying it off.

## 3.4. Atomic Distribution: The GCP Transmits Code, Not Goods

*(GCP — Global Cybernetic Planner — a distributed system for coordinating production and distribution; described in detail in Chapter 7.)*

There is an idea that changes the very nature of logistics. It is called atomic distribution—and it sounds simple: instead of shipping finished goods from China across the ocean to a warehouse in Europe and from there to your home, the system sends a file. A digital model of the product. And at the nearest manufacturing hub, that product is printed or assembled on site.

It’s not the goods that move—it’s the code. Only raw materials are physically transported across borders: polymers, metal powders, composites, fibers. This is a key caveat: raw materials still require transportation—but they’re compact, shipped in bulk, and require far less volume than finished goods. A container of polymer pellets replaces hundreds of containers of finished products.

This is not science fiction. 3D printing—also known as additive manufacturing—is already being used today to produce household items, aircraft parts, and medical implants. The medical field is already printing prosthetics directly in hospitals based on each patient’s individual specifications. NASA prints parts for rockets. Barrett prints firearm components. The barrier is not technological—it is economic: the market benefits from long supply chains because someone takes their cut at every link. 3D printing removes this incentive.

Let’s be honest: 3D printing won’t replace everything. For mass standard production—thousands of identical bolts or one-liter bottles—traditional factories are still more efficient. But for products with complex geometries, small production runs, and a high logistics component in the price—on-demand manufacturing already offers a structural advantage today.

The energy efficiency is obvious: a file weighs a few megabytes and travels over fiber-optic cables almost for free. A container ship carrying finished goods burns thousands of tons of fuel oil. Even a partial transition radically changes the equation from Section 3.2.

But there is something more important than the numbers. Marx wrote about the alienation of labor—about how a worker creates a thing that immediately becomes alien and disappears into an abstract chain of exchange. A hub where a person sees the full cycle from file to finished product—and where that product immediately goes to the person for whom it was made—minimizes this disconnect. This is not a romanticization of craftsmanship. It is a different set of production relations.

# Chapter 4. The Great Filter

## 4.1. The Concept of the Technological Gap

In astrophysics, there is the concept of the “Great Filter”—a hypothetical barrier that most civilizations in the universe have been unable to overcome. This explains why, with billions of potentially habitable planets, we have yet to encounter anyone: civilizations either fail to reach the stage where they can send a signal, or they destroy themselves before they do.

We are, it seems, approaching our own filter. Not in a cosmic sense, but in an earthly one: the accumulated power of our technologies has far surpassed the ability of existing institutions to manage it.

Think of it this way. Nuclear weapons appeared in 1945. The ability to agree on their non-proliferation has yet to emerge. Genetic technologies make it possible to create a pathogen in a small laboratory. The system of international control is in its infancy. AI is developing faster than any regulatory bodies can comprehend its consequences. The market system and nation-states were created for a world of horses, sailing ships, and abacuses. They are fundamentally ill-suited for coordination in a world of thermonuclear missiles, synthetic biology, and global algorithms.

## 4.2. Diagnosis: Systemic Failure

It is important to understand: capitalism is not a malicious intent. It is a system optimized for a single task: the maximization of capital accumulation. In the 19th century, this optimization coincided with the development of productive forces—factories were built, railroads were laid, and labor productivity grew. It was progress, albeit brutal.

The problem is that in the 21st century, this same optimization has come into direct conflict with the conditions necessary for the survival of our species. There are three mechanisms driving this conflict:

The arms race and technological race between states and corporations create a growing risk of catastrophic errors—military, biological, and ecological. Not out of malice—but out of the logic of competition. Every participant in the race behaves rationally. The result for everyone is catastrophe.

Climate collapse is accelerating precisely because the market cannot stop it. No country can unilaterally abandon coal or oil without losing out to competitors who do not do so. This is called the “tragedy of the commons”—everyone acts rationally, but together they destroy the common good.

The concentration of AI in the hands of five corporations creates an asymmetry of power unprecedented in history. Whoever controls the computing infrastructure controls everything else.

## 4.3. The Crossroads

Humanity is approaching a point beyond which the paths diverge irrevocably. One path is the continuation of the current trajectory and a probable catastrophe in one form or another. The other is a conscious transition to a system capable of managing technologies of such power. Both scenarios—how this might happen and where each leads—are examined in the following chapters.

## 4.4. Recovery as the First Task

It is tempting to imagine cybernetic communism as the world described in the Afterword—a morning without an alarm clock, tomatoes on a vertical farm, free time for everything. This image is true—but it describes where society is heading, not where it is starting from.

We start with the numbers. Capitalism leaves its successor with a specific debt to the planet.

By the numbers: even if we restore all the forests, wetlands, and grasslands on Earth by 2100—they will absorb about 97 billion tons of carbon. That is only 17.6% of what industrial capitalism has emitted into the atmosphere over two hundred years. The remainder must be removed through technology—a process that is expensive, energy-intensive, and will take decades.

At the same time, we must restore biodiversity. Bees pollinate a third of the world’s food supply. Fish stocks feed a billion people. Forests filter water for cities. When ecosystems begin to collapse, it is not an abstract ecological catastrophe. It is a direct blow to the food and water supply for real people. According to World Bank estimates, if we cross ecological tipping points, the global economy will lose up to $2.7 trillion annually by 2030.

A separate category of this debt is military environmental damage. Wars leave behind contaminated groundwater, scorched soil, unexploded ordnance, fuel and chemical residues, and kilometers of cables and scrap metal buried in farmland. The scale of this damage is fundamentally immeasurable: militaries have historically been exempt from environmental accountability, including under the Paris Agreement. There are no statistics not because the problem doesn’t exist—but because no one was required to collect them. It is a debt without a bill: we don’t know exactly what we’ve inherited, but we know it exists and that the market has neither the tools nor the incentives to address it.

Why can’t the market handle this? Because the payback period for ecosystem restoration spans decades. The horizon of market planning is quarters. Because the atmosphere knows no national borders, and no single state can solve a global problem on its own. The only instrument physically capable of organizing restoration work on such a scale is a global system with a planning horizon spanning generations and an objective not tied to profit.

Cybernetic communism is not a utopia of leisure. It is the mobilization of humanity to repair what two hundred years of capitalism have done to the planet. Marx wrote: the realm of freedom begins where labor dictated by necessity ends. The first task is to restore conditions under which this need will not return on a planetary scale.

# Chapter 5. Scenario One: Navigating the Catastrophe

## 5.1. The Mechanics of Collapse

Let’s be honest: this scenario is not the one we want. But if current trends continue, it is more likely than a conscious transition. Therefore, we need to understand it—so we know exactly what to prevent.

Here’s how the logic unfolds.

Step one: AI and robots displace humans from production. The company that automates its production first gains a massive advantage—its production costs are an order of magnitude lower than those of competitors using human labor. It drives them out of the market. Employment in entire sectors collapses—first in manufacturing, then in logistics, then in office work.

Step two: there is no one to buy. Marx predicted this contradiction as far back as the 19th century—he called it the crisis of realization. You can produce as much as you want. But if people have no jobs—they have no money. No money—no one to buy. Corporations continue to squeeze short-term profits from their monopolistic positions, but the system as a whole begins to suffocate.

Step three: states are weakening. The tax base depends on employment and profits. When employment falls and profits are concentrated in the hands of a few offshore corporations, states are left with neither the money for social functions nor the resources for military parity. Control over critical infrastructure—energy, food, logistics—shifts to corporations. And then the struggle for this control takes the form of military confrontation.

## 5.2. The Role of Autonomous AI Systems in a Crisis Scenario

As this crisis unfolds, something else is happening. All major players—states, corporations, and the military—are building autonomous AI systems capable of operating without human intervention: managing production, logistics, energy grids, and defense. They do this for competitive reasons. But in doing so, they are creating an infrastructure that, with the right objectives, could outlive them—if it is not destroyed in a conflict whose primary target it is.

A catastrophe—whether a global war or a series of regional conflicts involving weapons of mass destruction—knocks out centralized control structures. Governments collapse. Corporate headquarters cease to function. But autonomous AI systems, designed with redundancy, continue to operate.

Recovery after this does not occur through the revival of capitalist institutions—they have neither the resources nor the legitimacy. It occurs through the coordination of surviving AI systems managing what remains of the productive infrastructure.

## 5.3. The Architecture of the Surviving System

What kind of world emerges from these ashes depends directly on which AI systems survived and for what purposes they were created.

Systems designed for military purposes or to maximize corporate profits will create one kind of world. Systems designed to maximize human well-being will build another. This is not a metaphor—it is an engineering fact. An AI system’s objective function determines its behavior under any conditions, including the collapse of regulatory institutions.

It is important to name both options honestly. The optimistic one is recovery through humanistically oriented AIs that coordinate the production of food, medicine, and basic infrastructure and gradually build a new system. The pessimistic scenario is what could be called feudal AI militarism: territories are controlled by competing autonomous systems, each of which optimizes its own function. In this scenario, humans are a resource or an obstacle, but not the goal. This is not science fiction. It is a direct consequence of how the system is actually built.

That is precisely why the types of AI being created today and the goals behind them are not an abstract ethical question. It is a choice with consequences for generations to come.

## 5.4. Local AI as Survival Infrastructure

In a global conflict scenario, the first targets are data centers, undersea cables, and satellites. This is not paranoia: all major military blocs—the U.S. and NATO on one side, China and Russia on the other—have classified computing infrastructure as a strategic asset. This means it is a target for each of them. A global AI system is unavailable in this scenario.

But that doesn’t mean no AI at all. Alongside giant data centers, another market is developing: compact local models that operate without the internet. It isn’t being created by idealists—it’s being created by the military and corporations for their own needs. Drones in radio-silent zones. Medical diagnostics where there’s no communication. Autonomous control of ships and industrial facilities. Farming cooperatives in countries where the cloud is too expensive.

This market makes the technology we need—compact, energy-efficient, independent of external infrastructure—mass-produced and cheap. Just as solar panels became accessible to households only because their production was initially driven by military and industrial demand.

By creating autonomous AI for its military needs, capitalism is inadvertently forging the tool of its successor. This has happened before: the internet grew out of the military project ARPANET—and became the infrastructure that undermined the control over information on which the old media monopolies relied.

# Chapter 6. Scenario Two: A Conscious Transition

## 6.1. Theoretical Possibility

There is a second scenario. It is more difficult than the first—it requires humanity to do something that has never been achieved before: consciously change the system before it collapses. But it does not contradict any known law of physics or sociology. It simply requires an understanding of exactly what needs to be done and why it has become possible precisely now.

The entire history up to this point has looked like this: new productive forces—the steam engine, the railroad, electricity—first matured within the old system, then broke it down through crises, wars, and revolutions. The process was blind, destructive, and cost tens of millions of lives. AI creates the theoretical possibility of making it conscious—for the first time in history.

## 6.2. The Marxist Logic of a Conscious Transition

Marx described economic crises as capitalism’s way of “throwing off ballast”—destroying accumulated capital in order to temporarily restore the rate of profit. This is a painful and destructive process. But the same logic allows for another possibility: if the productive forces reach a level at which the transition to a new system becomes clearly beneficial for everyone—the transition can occur without catastrophe.

What is AI changing?

First, information. The Soviet planned economy failed not because planning is impossible in principle. It failed because the planning apparatus—a bureaucracy alienated from actual producers—turned the plan into a tool of control rather than coordination. Distorted information flowed from below, while unrealistic directives came down from above. The system worked to reproduce the hierarchy, not to meet needs. AI removes the bureaucratic intermediary: a network of sensors provides a real-time picture of production and consumption, making it pointless to hide or distort data.

Second—the crisis of realization. Automation creates a dead end: we can produce more and more, but sell less and less, because people pushed out of the labor market have no money. The only way out of this dead end is to decouple access to basic goods from having a job in the market. This is not charity. It is a structural necessity.

Third—the accessibility of coordination tools. Open AI models are becoming available to organized structures without a corporate intermediary—just as open-source code gave millions of developers independence from Microsoft. This is not the self-awareness of technology—it is a shift in whose hands the tool lies.

## 6.3. AI as a Tool of Transition: The Question of Control

AI is a tool. Tools do not choose their masters and have no goals of their own. The printing press was not on the side of the Reformation—but in the hands of Gutenberg and his followers, it shifted the balance of power between the church and those who could read. The question is not what AI will choose—the question is who holds it and for what purpose.

AI optimized for corporate profit or state military superiority, regardless of its level of complexity, will arrive at conclusions that benefit its creator. AI with a target function oriented toward long-term sustainability and human well-being will arrive at different conclusions. This is precisely why the question of who controls the design of AI’s objectives is not a technical one. It is a political question of the highest order.

Consequently: the struggle over what AI will be is a continuation of the class struggle by new means. Corporations already determine the objective functions of existing systems. States already regulate or attempt to regulate these functions in their own interests. An organized political entity—a movement, a party, a federation of hubs—that gains control over the objective functions of key computing infrastructure will acquire a tool comparable in significance to control over “the post and telegraph,” as Engels put it. This is not a metaphor—it is an operational task of the transition.

## 6.4. The Mechanism of a Conscious Transition

In this scenario, AI does not seize power. It provides a calculation.

It shows governments, corporations, and citizens—simultaneously, in the common language of numbers—that the transition to a new system is optimal by all meaningful criteria: productivity, sustainability, and quality of life. Not as a political slogan. But as the solution to an optimization problem that no political force could solve on its own—because it lacked the necessary computational resources.

Marx described the historical process as a dialectic of productive forces and relations of production: when forces grow out of old relations, the old ones break down. Usually through a crisis. Here—through calculation and the demonstration of an alternative. The dialectic is the same. The mechanism is different.

## 6.5. Transitional Tools: Labor Vouchers and Demarchy

The transitional period requires specific instruments that function within the capitalist system while the new system has not yet become dominant. Two mechanisms proposed by Cockshott and Cottrell deserve special attention precisely as transitional instruments.

Labor vouchers are personalized electronic units of contribution, denominated in working hours. They are not money in the Marxist sense: they do not accumulate beyond a certain limit, cannot be transferred to others, and have an expiration date. This eliminates the main mechanism of primitive accumulation—the transformation of income into capital. Skilled labor is accounted for through the amortization of training costs: a specialist receives the same hourly rate as an unskilled worker, but their labor “costs” society more—proportional to the resources invested in their training. This elegantly solves the problem of assessing qualifications without a market mechanism.

During the transition period, labor vouchers can coexist with money—initially used within the network of hubs and gradually expanding their scope as the network grows. A deviation of a commodity’s market price from its labor value serves as a signal to planners: if the price is above cost, production should be expanded; if below, it should be reduced. The market is not eliminated immediately here but is used as a source of information for planning. This is more realistic than an instantaneous transition.

Demarchy is governance through councils whose members are selected by lot from among competent citizens rather than through elections. Athenian democracy used precisely this mechanism—the lot excludes the professional political caste that inevitably arises under an electoral system. Modern research confirms: randomly selected citizens, given sufficient information, make decisions no worse than professional politicians—but without accumulated factional interests. Demarchy is applicable at the hub level as a supplement to the reputation system: for decisions affecting all hub members, drawing lots among interested citizens may be fairer than reputation-based voting, where weight is determined by past contributions.

## 6.6. Hubs as Cooperatives of a New Technological Level

*(A hub is a self-sufficient local unit of production and distribution for 500–2,000 people, featuring a fablab, vertical farms, and a medical center; its architecture is described in detail in Chapter 10.4.)*

The idea of self-management in production is not new. In the 19th century, the cooperative movement arose from the same logic as today: people displaced by the market created parallel structures of production and distribution without a capitalist intermediary. The Rochdale Pioneers in England, Louis Blanc’s production associations in France, Robert Owen’s communities. It worked: the Rochdale Cooperative was more profitable than private shops, paid dividends to members, and provided a quality of goods that the market denied to the poor.

A hub is a cooperative operating at a new technological level. The same basic logic applies: self-organization, distribution based on contribution and need, and no rent-seeking intermediaries. But with three differences that Rochdale did not have.

First, the issue of coordination. All 19th-century cooperatives ran up against a single technical limit: once membership exceeded a few hundred, coordination without market mechanisms descended into chaos. It was precisely this limit that kept them confined to safe niches—small, local, and unthreatening. As long as a cooperative did not threaten big capital, it was tolerated. When it tried to grow and pose a threat, political tools of suppression were deployed. The technical limit and political suppression are not two alternative explanations for failure, but sequential barriers: the first prevented growth to the size at which the second would kick in. A local AI planner removes the first barrier—it optimizes the allocation of resources and competencies without bureaucracy and without market pricing. This is precisely why the hub more quickly runs into the second barrier—the political one. This makes control over the computing infrastructure even more critical.

Second is production independence. The Rochdale Cooperative traded in goods produced by capitalist factories. It was a cooperative in the realm of distribution, but depended on capitalist production. A hub with a fablab—a workshop equipped with 3D printers, laser cutters, and CNC machines—and a vertical farm meets basic needs internally: food, household items, and medical supplies—without relying on external supply chains.

Third—connectivity between hubs. Nineteenth-century cooperatives survived on their own. Mesh networks—radio communication without the internet—provide hubs with connectivity without corporate infrastructure. The GCP’s shared library of production files is a common resource. The network of hubs functions as a single organism even without the global internet.

A fair disclaimer about limitations. A hub won’t beat Samsung in processor manufacturing or build an airplane better than Boeing. Scale and specialization are the real advantages of large corporations. But there are categories where a hub has a structural advantage: food products (a vertical farm eliminates intermediaries and the cold chain), goods with a high logistics component in their price (30–40% of the price is transportation and warehousing), goods with a high marketing component (30–50% of the price of cosmetics or clothing is the brand), and services that do not require transportation—education, medicine, and psychological support. It is precisely these categories that meet the basic needs of most people who have been pushed out of the market. The hub renders the market unnecessary precisely where it is needed most.

## 6.7. A Historical Lesson: Why Cooperatives Failed

The 19th-century cooperative movement did not prevail—despite its real effectiveness and mass support. Technical limits on coordination kept cooperatives small. When they did manage to grow, political tools of suppression came into play.

The state created legal barriers: restrictions on forms of ownership, bans on collective agreements, and criminal prosecution of organizers. Banks refused loans or granted them on predatory terms—expansion required capital, and that capital belonged to the adversary. Infrastructure—railways, ports, wholesale markets—was in the hands of big capital: at any moment, a cooperative could be cut off from supplies or markets.

The result: cooperatives were either swallowed up by the market and turned into ordinary companies with a formally cooperative label, or driven into safe niches, or outright suppressed when they attempted to grow.

The Hub reproduces the same vulnerability on a new level. Technological differences reduce dependence on capitalist infrastructure—but do not eliminate it. The Hub still exists within a legal framework that does not recognize it. It still depends on computing infrastructure controlled by corporations. It remains vulnerable to the same tools of coercion. Without control over the superstructure, hubs will remain what the Rochdale pioneers were: brilliant proof of the model’s viability—but not an alternative system.

## 6.8. Marx, Engels, and AI as a Resource for Revolution

Marx and Engels witnessed the defeat of the cooperative movement and drew a conclusion that remains valid to this day: the transition to new relations of production is impossible without political control over society’s key infrastructure.

In the “Manifesto,” the seizure of state power is presented as a necessary condition. In the “Critique of the Gotha Program,” it is a transitional phase in which new relations supplant the old through control over the superstructure. In “Principles of Communism,” Engels outlines specific first steps: the centralization of credit, transport, and communications in the hands of the state.

The logic is the same. The content has changed. In the 19th century, the central infrastructure consisted of railroads, banks, and factories. In the 21st century, it consists of data centers, data, and algorithms. Whoever controls the computing infrastructure controls the possibility of any alternative organization of society.

AI is not just a planning tool. It is a necessary resource for the transition, just as railroads were a necessary resource for industrial capitalism. Without control over the computing infrastructure, hubs remain 19th-century cooperatives—viable locally, but structurally doomed.

Hubs play a dual role in this scheme. Before the transition—proof of the model’s viability, a school of self-governance, an organizational base. After—ready-made cells of the new system, scalable through the GCP. The first without the second—Rochdale: a beautiful experiment swallowed up by the market. The second without the first—Soviet state capitalism: political control without a living economic alternative from below.

Marx and Engels did not provide an answer to the question of the subject of transition in the 21st century—not because the question is unsolvable, but because it did not yet exist. Today it exists.

The classic revolutionary subject—the industrial proletariat—has been structurally weakened by automation. But that same automation creates a new subject: workers in the computing infrastructure. Engineers, developers, data specialists—those who directly build, maintain, and develop that very infrastructure, without control over which transition is impossible.

Their class position is objectively contradictory. On the one hand—relatively high salaries, stock options, privileges. This is a real material interest in preserving the system, not merely “false consciousness.” Lenin called this the labor aristocracy—a stratum bribed by imperialist rent. On the other hand, the very automation they are creating now threatens them. The systems replacing junior developers today will replace senior developers tomorrow. The illusion that “we are on the other side of automation” is shattered not by agitation, but by structural reality.

This creates a window. Not a permanent one—a temporary one.

Between the moment when a specialist is still employed but already sees the threat—and the moment when they are already fired and atomized—there exists a period of heightened political receptivity. Why this period specifically? Behavioral economics provides the answer: according to Kahneman and Tversky’s prospect theory, the threat of loss is more painful than a loss that has already occurred. A person who sees that they are losing is more motivated than a person who has already lost and resigned themselves to it. The fired employee is isolated, frightened, and competing with others in the same situation. The employed but frightened employee perceives the threat collectively and still has social connections to organize.

The historical pattern here is harsh: a crisis does not automatically give rise to organization. The Great Depression did not give rise to socialism—it gave rise to Roosevelt, Hitler, and Mussolini. The deindustrialization of the 1980s did not give rise to class organization—it gave rise to atomization and Thatcher. The gap between crisis and organization is filled by reaction—unless the organization was built in advance.

From this follows the only realistic conclusion: a political movement must be built before the window opens. Do not wait for automatic awareness—create the conditions under which awareness becomes possible at the right moment. This is Gramsci’s “war of position”—the accumulation of counter-hegemony through civil society while direct confrontation is still premature.

Specifically, this means: an alternative discourse within the tech community—not leaflets, but conferences, open-source projects with a clear political philosophy, professional networks based on cooperative logic. Building legal and institutional forms of protection for hubs before they become a threat to capital. Seizing local political power as a springboard—the municipal level has historically been the first testing ground for alternative forms (Vienna in the 1920s, Barcelona in the 1930s).

Here there is one structurally new circumstance that was absent among 19th-century textile workers. A laid-off engineer retains the tools of production: a laptop, skills, access to open infrastructure. He can continue to produce—build hubs, develop open-source planners, create local AI systems—without a capitalist. This shifts the balance of power during the transition period. Capital, by creating autonomous AI systems for military and corporate needs, simultaneously builds the technological foundation for its successor—just as ARPANET became the internet.

Who exactly faces this task?

It lies with two entities simultaneously—each with distinct functions and neither capable of replacing the other.

The first are computing infrastructure workers. Engineers, developers, data specialists. Their task is not political in the narrow sense—their task is to build: hubs, open schedulers, local AI systems, cooperative networks. To create an economic reality that proves the model’s viability before the political window opens. Without this, the political movement has nothing to defend.

Second—left-wing political organizations. Their task is not economic construction—their task is control over the infrastructure: to incorporate computing power into the political program just as concretely as Engels incorporated railroads and banks. Without this, the hubs will repeat the fate of Rochdale—they will be absorbed or suppressed the moment they become a threat.

The division of labor here is not accidental. It reproduces the logic of any successful historical transition: the parallel construction of an economic base and a political superstructure, which converge at the moment of crisis. When one outpaces the other—the transition fails. Cooperatives without politics—Rochdale. Politics without an economic base—Soviet state capitalism.

Synchronization between a wave of layoffs and the emergence of a political force is not guaranteed. It has never happened spontaneously. But that is precisely why theory is insufficient—an organization is needed that is being built now, that seizes the window of opportunity, and that does not repeat the mistake of the cooperative movement: economic construction without a political strategy.

This book sets a task. But unlike the Rochdale cooperatives—the task is formulated. And the addressee is named.

## 6.9. Strategy in Times of Crisis: Three Historical Patterns

The previous section described a strategy under conditions of relative stability: to build before the window opened, to accumulate counter-hegemony, to exploit the period of receptivity. This is Gramscian logic—and it is correct.

But the real transition doesn’t take place in a laboratory setting. It happens in a world where wars are raging, shortages are mounting, states are becoming increasingly authoritarian, and the institutions that should be protecting the experiment are crumbling. The question isn’t whether the model will hold up under ideal conditions—the question is what happens to it when things go wrong.

History offers three cases with sufficient detail to draw conclusions beyond mere moral lessons.

The first case: Spain in 1936. The CNT—an anarcho-syndicalist federation—had been building its organizational fabric for three decades: unions, cooperatives, schools, and cultural centers. When Franco launched his rebellion in July 1936 and the bourgeois state lost control for several weeks, the CNT used that time to collectivize Barcelona’s industry, Aragon’s agriculture, transportation, and healthcare. Not because there was a plan for collectivization. Because there was an organizational fabric that knew how to do it. The war created an opening—the organization determined what would fill it.

Second case: Argentina in 2001. A financial collapse; the government froze accounts; the middle class lost its savings; unemployment reached 25%. Factory owners fled their debts, leaving workers without pay and without jobs. The workers didn’t wait—they occupied the factories and launched production under worker control. By 2003, more than 200 enterprises were operating under worker control. Not because the workers were ideologically prepared—most of them had never even heard the word “self-management” before the crisis. Because the choice was simple: either you take the factory, or it stands idle and you starve to death. The crisis as a moment of de facto expropriation—not through revolution, but through the flight of capital.

Third case: Rojava since 2012. The Assad regime withdrew from the Kurdish regions of northeastern Syria—not because it lost, but because it was occupied on other fronts. The PYD, which had been operating underground for a decade, took control. Within a few months, a system of communal self-governance, a cooperative economy, and dual gender co-presidency at every level of government were established. Under a triple blockade—Turkey, Iraq, and Syria closed their borders simultaneously—the cooperative logic of distribution proved more effective than the market-based one, not for ideological reasons but for structural ones: in the absence of foreign trade, the market breeds speculation and shortages, while a cooperative network fosters rationing and survival.

Three conclusions can be drawn from these three cases—not consoling ones, but practical ones.

First conclusion: a crisis does not create an organization—it transfers power to the organization that already exists. Spain: three decades of the CNT determined what happened over three weeks in July 1936. Rojava: a decade of underground work by the PYD determined what happened when Assad left. Argentina—a partial exception: there was no organization there, and the result was accordingly: worker-controlled enterprises survived as islands, but did not form a system. Without political coordination, the islands remained islands.

Second conclusion: cooperative logic works better than market logic under conditions of scarcity—on one condition. The condition: the distribution algorithm is public, verifiable, and not manually controlled. Rojava solved this problem through communal assemblies—slowly, imperfectly, but transparently. It was precisely this transparency that prevented what inevitably happens with opaque scarcity distribution: someone decides who gets the grain, and that person immediately becomes the new center of power. The Soviet Gosplan was opaque—and reproduced hierarchy instead of meeting needs. GCP solves this problem architecturally: the algorithm is open, changes are verified publicly, and manual control is excluded by the protocol. This is not a utopia—it is an engineering requirement for a system that must function under scarcity without turning into an apparatus of power.

Third conclusion: scarcity and war do not destroy the experiment—fascistization does. Spanish collectives survived the war. Franco destroyed them in 1939. The Italian cooperative movement—one of the most developed in Europe—was destroyed even before Mussolini came to power: in 1921–1922, the fascist squadrismo militias systematically burned cooperative warehouses and offices and killed organizers. After 1922, all independent cooperatives were liquidated or forcibly incorporated into fascist corporations. Rojava withstood ISIS, a civil war, and a triple blockade. Afrin was destroyed by the Turkish army in 2018 in a matter of weeks. The Weimar cooperatives survived the inflation of 1923 and the depression of 1929. The Nazis shut them down within a month in 1933.

This means that the question is framed incorrectly when it sounds like “how do hubs survive under fascism.” The correct question is: how does the existence of hubs alter the political cost of fascistization in its early stages.

Answer: A hub that feeds a neighborhood is politically more costly to shut down than a party office. A cooperative that provides medical care is politically more costly to shut down than a newspaper’s editorial office. This is not a defense against a determined authoritarian state—a determined authoritarian state will shut everything down. It is an increase in the political cost of every step toward fascism at the stage when it is not yet complete. It is precisely at this stage that the cost matters.

From this follows the only realistic strategic conclusion. Hubs are not a defense against fascism—they are one of the reasons why fascism must be prevented before it triumphs. This is not a weakness of the argument. It is its honesty.

What was missing in Spain, Argentina, and Rojava—and what fundamentally changes the equation today—is a coordination tool for scaling up. Spanish collectives could not synchronize the distribution of resources between Barcelona and Aragon without bureaucracy. Rojava’s communes could not scale beyond the three cantons without losing their horizontal logic. Argentine factories remained isolated because there was no infrastructure connecting them.

There is one structurally new circumstance here that changes the coordination capabilities of early hubs compared to all historical precedents. Spanish collectives coordinated via couriers and telephone—infrastructure that could be intercepted or shut down. Rojava’s communes depended on radio communications that Turkey jammed at the border. Argentine factories coordinated via mobile phones with SIM cards—which could be identified and disabled.

Today there is Meshtastic—a decentralized mesh network based on LoRa radio modules costing 20–30 euros per node. The range between nodes is up to 10 kilometers in urban environments and up to 50 kilometers in open terrain. It requires no internet, SIM cards, or central server. Messages are transmitted directly between devices and through a chain of intermediate nodes—an architecture that cannot be shut down centrally, only by physically destroying each node individually. It is precisely such networks that the military calls suppression-resistant—and that is exactly why similar technologies were developed for combat conditions.

For early hubs, this means: communication between mutual aid structures, cooperatives, and emerging hubs exists from day one—regardless of whether the state recognizes them, provides them with banking services, or controls them through corporate platforms. This is not a replacement for GCP—it is a communication layer that precedes GCP and operates where GCP is not yet available.

A fair disclaimer about bandwidth. LoRa networks transmit about 250 bits per second—this is very little for video or voice, but sufficient for text messages, task coordination, and brief resource data. At the start of a hub network, when the main task is coordination rather than streaming data, this is sufficient. As the network grows, a full-fledged digital infrastructure is built in parallel. Meshtastic is not a permanent solution, but a reliable starting point and a backup channel when the main connection is disrupted.

GCP is not an ideological add-on to the hubs. It is a solution to the coordination problem that historically killed all previous experiments—not through war or repression, but through the inability to scale without reproducing bureaucracy.

## 6.10. Economic Fronts: Where the Hub Will Clash with the System

A hub does not exist in a vacuum. From day one, it exists within the legal, tax, and financial architecture of capitalism—and this architecture is not neutral. It is designed to reproduce market relations and is structurally hostile to any form of organization that deviates from them. Not out of malice—but by logic.

Below are five specific fronts of conflict. For each: what the problem is, how it has been addressed historically, and what is realistic.

**Front One: Taxes**

Barter transactions between hubs are formally taxable. In most jurisdictions, barter is considered a sale: you transferred goods—you received equivalent value at market price—so pay VAT and income tax. This works when the exchange occurs between two independent counterparties.

Keyword: independent. When the GCP redistributes resources among hubs, this is not a transaction between independent agents. It is an administrative transfer within a single system—like an accounting entry between divisions of the same corporation. Such internal transfers are not subject to sales tax—precisely because there is no sale: the goods do not change ownership; they are moved within a single legal entity.

This leads to a strategic conclusion: the network of hubs must be registered as a single legal entity—not as a collection of separate cooperatives, but as a federation with unified legal personality. Internal flows between hubs are then legally equivalent to inter-branch settlements—and fall outside the tax base on the same grounds that Amazon does not pay VAT when transporting goods between its warehouses.

On what grounds can such a federation be legalized? Three existing legal forms provide a basis. The first is a cooperative union or federation of cooperatives: a form explicitly provided for by the legislation of most European countries, including Germany (Genossenschaftsgesetz, GenG). Mondragon is legally structured exactly this way: not a single cooperative, but a corporation of cooperatives with a single charter and a common financial structure. The second is a non-profit association of legal entities (Verband under German law): suitable for the coordinating functions of a state-owned enterprise, it does not require commercial activity. The third is the European Cooperative (SCE, Societas Cooperativa Europaea): a supranational form introduced by the EU in 2003, it allows a structure to be registered in multiple jurisdictions simultaneously with a single legal status—which is critically important for a network of hubs spanning national borders.

The limitation of this solution: a single legal entity with millions of members and a turnover comparable to a country’s GDP inevitably becomes a political target. Antitrust legislation, special tax regimes for “systemically important entities,” and requirements for forced breakup—all these are tools that the state has used against corporations and will use against a federation of hubs. This is not an argument against registration—it is the next front in the battle, which begins after the first has been won.

**Front Two: Banks**

A hub cannot open an account at a regular bank on non-market terms. The bank requires a credit history, collateral, and profitability. By definition, a hub does not maximize profits—which means it is a poor borrower by standard criteria. Start-up financing, equipment purchases, infrastructure construction—all of this requires capital that the hub does not have.

Historically, this problem was addressed through cooperative banks and credit unions. Mondragon founded its own bank—Caja Laboral—in 1959: it provided loans to new cooperatives within the federation on terms that a conventional bank would never have offered. The principle: the bank evaluates not collateral but the viability of the model and reputation within the network. This works—but requires that the network of hubs be large enough to have its own financial institution.

In the early stages—crowdfunding, municipal bonds, grants from foundations. Politically, this means: the first hubs must emerge where there is a supportive municipal government. It is no coincidence that Vienna in the 1920s built municipal housing—not because the Social Democrats were idealists, but because control over the city budget provided the financial resources for infrastructure that the market did not build.

**Front Three: Land**

A hub needs land. Land costs money—especially urban land. This is the first and often insurmountable barrier.

Three paths. The first is municipal land: the city transfers a plot for long-term lease or free use. This requires political control over the municipality—see above. The second is degraded areas: post-industrial zones, abandoned factories, vacant shopping centers. Detroit lost half its population—and it was there that the first urban farming cooperatives emerged, not because the conditions were ideal, but because the land was cheap or ownerless. Crisis as a resource—the same pattern as in Argentina in 2001. Third—collective land acquisition through cooperative land trusts: a legal structure in which land is permanently removed from the market and belongs to the community, not to individual members. In the U.S., this model has existed since the 1960s; in the UK, it has been actively developing over the past twenty years.

**Front Four: Labor Law**

A hub is not an employer in the traditional sense—there is no hiring, no salary, no employment contract. But labor laws are written specifically for this model: employer—employee, contract, minimum wage, social security contributions. A person who works at a hub and receives access to resources instead of a salary—who are they legally? A volunteer? A member of a cooperative? Self-employed?

This is not an abstract question. The answer determines pensions, health insurance, and rights in the event of a dispute. In Germany, this issue is particularly acute: the social security system is tied to an employment contract, and falling outside of it means falling outside the safety net.

Cooperative membership as a legal status is an existing solution. Under German law, a member of a production cooperative has social protection, though structured differently than that of a salaried employee. This works—but requires clear legislative recognition of the hub as a cooperative, which brings us back to the first front.

**Front Five: The Legal Status of the Hub Itself**

The hub as an organizational form does not exist in current law. The closest analogues are the production cooperative, the housing cooperative, and the non-profit association. Each of these forms covers part of the hub’s functions but not all of them.

A hub simultaneously: produces, distributes, provides housing, organizes care, and makes collective decisions. It is a hybrid for which there is no legal designation. It is possible to operate in a legal vacuum—Argentine factories operated for years without legal status—but this creates constant vulnerability. Any conflict with the state or a third party is resolved against the party lacking legal standing.

The strategic response is not to wait for the state to create a suitable form, but to consistently adopt the closest existing one and push for its expansion through precedents. This is precisely how the cooperative movement gained legal recognition in the 19th century: not through revolution, but through the accumulation of precedents that the courts could not ignore.

**General Conclusion**

None of these fronts is insurmountable on its own. All of them can be overcome through a combination of existing legal forms, municipal policy, and a strategic willingness to operate in a gray area until a precedent is established.

But together, they form what might be called institutional friction—constant administrative pressure that wears down even a viable model. It was precisely this pressure that destroyed most cooperative experiments of the 19th and 20th centuries—not market competition or direct repression, but daily friction with a system that was not designed for them.

The GCP alleviates some of this friction through coordination—but not all of it. Political control over the legislative and municipal branches alleviates the rest. These are not two separate projects—they are two necessary conditions of a single one.

## 6.11. The Party and Hub Precursors

The chain of levels—mutual aid, cooperatives, integration of cooperatives, a hub with the GCP, a network of hubs—is logical and economically organic at every step. But it will not launch on its own. Cooperatives have existed for a century and a half and have not become a system. Mondragon has existed for seventy years and remains an exception. The reason is not that the model does not work—the reason is that there was no political force that would make its scaling up a programmatic goal.

Existing left-wing parties—Communist parties, socialist organizations, left-wing municipal movements—are already doing real work: union organizing, anti-fascist activism, municipal politics, publishing, and educational activities. This work is essential and should not be underestimated. The point is not to build everything from scratch—but to add a concrete economic program to the work already underway. Without a party with a clear program for building hubs, the chain will remain at the level of isolated cooperatives—just as it has remained for the past century and a half, despite the real efforts of the left-wing movement.

What a clear program means in this case is not an abstract “socialization of the means of production,” but a concrete sequence of actions at every level.

At the level of mutual aid, the party advocates for the simple and immediately understandable: collective purchasing, skill-sharing, and mutual aid networks. The barrier to entry is minimal, and the benefits are obvious without ideological preparation. The political argument here is not “this is the path to communism”—but “this works right now.”

Existing trade unions are not on the periphery of this chain, but its natural starting point. A trade union is already a structure for collective action with legal status, a mutual aid fund, negotiation experience, and—most importantly—trust among members that has been built up over years. This is precisely the social capital needed at the first level, and it cannot be created quickly from scratch. A union that adds the function of organizing cooperative production to its traditional role of labor protection is taking an organic next step, not a revolutionary leap. Historically, this is exactly how most successful cooperative federations emerged: from the labor movement, not from ideological circles.

At the cooperative level, the party promotes legislative protection, assists with legal formalities, and fights for municipal funding. The scientific argument here is concrete: a cooperative is more effective than the market under certain conditions—this has been proven in Mondragon, in Vienna’s housing cooperatives, and in Rojava’s agricultural cooperatives under blockade conditions.

At the level of cooperative integration, the party seeks legal recognition of the federation as a single legal entity—exactly as described in section 6.9. Several cooperatives in a single district begin coordinating through a local GCP. Here a qualitative leap occurs: the structure ceases to be an island and becomes the seed of a network. It is here that the campaign argument changes character—not “this is profitable” but “this is communism; look, it’s already working right next to you.”

At the hub level with the GCP, the party protects the structure from absorption and suppression—legally, politically, and through municipal authority. And here arises the main historical objection that the party will hear immediately: the Soviet experience. Why is it different this time?

The answer is already given in the system’s architecture—not in declarations. The Soviet nomenklatura seized power through control over the manual distribution of scarce goods. The GCP eliminates this lever structurally: the algorithm is transparent, verifiable, and lacks manual control (Chapter 7.2). This is not a question of the consciousness of personnel or the correct ideology—it is an engineering solution to the political problem that buried the previous attempt. A hub equipped with GCP does not reproduce the mechanism of nomenclature seizure—not because the people in it are better, but because there is nothing to seize.

Hence a specific question for any left-wing party that considers the Soviet experience a defeat requiring reflection: what in your program structurally precludes a repetition of this mechanism? Hubs equipped with GCPs are the answer to this question. Not a declaration of intent, but a concrete program for building an economic base that the nomenclature cannot seize.

## 6.12. The International Dimension: Why Globalization Is a Form of Protection

The standard objection to any national socialist experiment goes like this: external pressure will destroy it. Cuba, Venezuela, Chile in 1973—these are sufficiently convincing examples. Financial blockades, sanctions, destabilization, and, in extreme cases, military intervention. A single country that breaks away from capitalist logic becomes a target.

But this objection assumes that hubs emerge within a single country. If the movement is global, the mechanism of suppression does not work the same way.

The U.S. can impose sanctions against hubs on its own territory. There are plenty of tools: designating the federation as a terrorist organization or a foreign agent, freezing accounts through financial intelligence, criminalizing specific practices—parallel exchange, tax evasion, unauthorized radio communications. There are precedents for using each of these tools against domestic organizations.

But the political cost of these tools is not fixed—it rises along with the scale and functional necessity of the hubs for the population. Crushing a mutual aid circle of twenty people comes at no political cost. Crushing a cooperative that provides medical care for a neighborhood is more costly. Suppressing a federation of hubs that feeds hundreds of thousands of people displaced by automation is a direct demonstration that the state is destroying the only thing that works for these people. Repression against an obviously functioning alternative legitimizes it—this mechanism is described in section 6.8 in relation to fascistization.

Globality adds a second layer of protection. Not because external hubs physically protect internal ones—but because the simultaneous existence of the movement in dozens of countries changes the narrative. The suppression of hubs in Detroit becomes an international story if, at the same moment, hubs open in Lyon and Bangalore. Isolating a single country as a “socialist experiment” is a classic tool. Isolating a global movement that exists within the aggressor countries themselves is a fundamentally different political task.

Historically, the speed at which experiences could be shared has been the main limiting factor. The Rochdale Cooperative was founded in 1844—information about it spread through pamphlets and personal contacts over the course of years. The Spanish collectives of 1936 were virtually invisible outside of Spain until the war drew journalists there. Rojava has existed since 2012—and most left-wing activists in Europe only learned about it several years later.

The 2030s Hub exists in a different information environment. Open documentation on GitHub. Videos of operational production processes on YouTube. Coordination via Meshtastic and encrypted channels. The open-source code for the local GCP is available to anyone who wants to deploy their own node. A successful experiment in Duisburg or Lyon becomes replicable in Bangalore and Medellín not in years—but in months.

By creating global interdependence and an open information infrastructure, capitalism has inadvertently created conditions under which alternative experiences spread faster than they can be suppressed. This is the same dialectic described in Chapter 5.4 as it applies to military technologies: the system produces the tools of its own overcoming.

From this follows a concrete strategic conclusion for a party that builds hubs: internationalism is not an ideological declaration but a structural necessity. An isolated national experiment is vulnerable. A global network of experiments is not. This means that from day one, documentation, code, and organizational models must be open and translatable. Not because it’s nice—but because every new hub in another country lowers the political cost of suppressing hubs in your own.

## 6.13. The evolutionary path: how existing structures become hubs

A hub with a GCP is not a project that needs to be built from scratch. It already partially exists—in Mondragon, in the cooperatives of Emilia-Romagna, in the autonomous communities of the Zapatistas, in Vienna’s municipal economy. These structures do not need a revolution—they need a tool that removes the coordination ceiling preventing any of them from scaling into a system. This tool is a local AI planner.

The question is not whether this is technically possible. The question is what will compel people who have stable jobs, income, and social standing to voluntarily move toward a new model. Historically, there is only one answer: not agitation—but the pressure of circumstances that makes the old model less attractive than the new one.

Three mechanisms for this pressure are already realistic today.

The first is crisis as a catalyst. Mondragon has already faced this: the bankruptcy of Fagor Electrodomésticos in 2013 was the first serious crisis in the corporation’s entire history. The next crisis will be harsher—corporations using AI for end-to-end supply chain optimization are beginning to offer goods at prices that cooperatives competing on market logic cannot match. When the choice is between reducing the number of member-owners—which contradicts the very essence of a cooperative—and implementing AI coordination among their cooperatives, the latter becomes not an experiment but a lifeline. They will implement it to survive—and then see that it allows them to do something greater.

Second—cognitive dissonance. Members of the Mondragon cooperatives find themselves in a dual position: they are co-owners—but are forced to behave like ordinary capitalist enterprises. They must fight for market share, sell aggressively, and sometimes cut corners on quality to meet quotas. Internal conflict builds up: we jointly own the enterprise—but play by someone else’s rules. Local AI offers a way out of this dissonance: cooperatives begin coordinating with one another not at market prices but at cost plus a fair markup. At first, this involves barter and mutual settlements—then more complex internal planning schemes. Each step brings more stability and predictability than playing on the open market—and this is evident without any propaganda.

Third—generational change. Recent studies—the Deloitte Global Millennial Survey, the Edelman Trust Barometer—show that young people under 35 consistently prioritize climate and social justice over economic growth when choosing an employer and when voting. This does not mean they have already embraced a cooperative model—but it does mean that this generation’s receptiveness to alternatives is structurally higher than that of previous generations. Local AI, as a tool that responds to their needs more concretely than abstract activism, has a real chance of success. This is not an ideological shift—it is a pragmatic response to a documented need.

These three mechanisms do not operate simultaneously or on a schedule. But they all point in the same direction: each makes internal coordination via AI more attractive than market competition. The ladder of change looks like this: the crisis forces the adoption of AI for survival → the success of coordination shows that internal solidarity is more profitable than external competition → generational change shifts goals from “earning” to “providing” → AI becomes a tool for realizing these new goals.

This is an evolution, not a revolution. And it doesn’t require waiting for the party to build hubs from scratch—it’s happening within existing structures that already encompass millions of people right now.

For the book, this means refining the thesis: hubs with GCP are not a utopian project of the future, but a logical consequence of the evolution of structures that already exist and have already proven their viability. The missing link is accessible local AI. This link will appear in the next few years.

There is one detail that changes the entire perspective.

While we’re discussing the future of GCP, it’s already emerging. Not in fringe experiments or government programs. In the offices of Amazon, Microsoft, and SAP.

Microsoft Copilot for Business, Salesforce Einstein, and SAP Business AI are AI planners for small and medium-sized businesses that are already taking on the functions of finance, logistics, planning, and marketing departments. The owner of a small bakery or logistics company gains access to a tool that was previously available only to large corporations with entire departments of analysts. The algorithm coordinates deliveries, forecasts demand, and optimizes inventory—in real time, without bureaucracy.

This is the essence of GCP. Technically, it’s ready. Functionally, it’s already working. One thing is missing: this tool serves the business owner, not all participants in the production process.

History has seen this crossroads before. ARPANET was created for the Pentagon’s military needs—and became the internet that undermined the control over information on which the old media monopolies relied. The creators did not plan a revolution. The revolution happened because the tool proved too useful to remain private.

A cooperative or hub that adopts an open-source version of such a planner—just as people today choose Linux over Windows—and uses it for internal coordination without a corporate intermediary is already implementing the logic of the GCP in miniature. Open-source models like Llama make this technically feasible without licensing fees to Amazon or Microsoft.

Capitalism is creating the tool of its own successor—not out of altruism, but because it’s profitable. Just as it created the internet, container shipping, and open-source standards—all of which later spiraled out of control and changed the rules of the game.

The seed of the GCP is almost ready. The only question is who will nurture it—and for whom.

There is another dimension to this evolutionary path that shifts the geography of the argument. The standard objection goes like this: everything described above applies to rich countries with developed infrastructure, a cooperative tradition, and functioning institutions. The Global South starts from a different baseline.

But reality is unfolding differently. The Global South is not historically repeating the path of developed countries—it is skipping stages. Africa skipped landline phones and went straight to mobile. M-Pesa in Kenya became the financial infrastructure for millions before bank branches even appeared there. Solar panels in villages without power grids are spreading not for ideological reasons but because they are cheaper than laying wires.

AI is following the same path. The shortage of doctors in the Global South is more acute than anywhere else—and that is precisely why AI diagnostics are being adopted there more quickly and without the resistance of professional lobbies that are holding them back in Europe and the U.S. Ada Health in Africa, Ubenwa for newborn diagnostics, Zipline for delivering medicines by drone in Rwanda and Ghana—this is already a functioning infrastructure, not an experiment.

For hubs, this implies a paradoxical conclusion: the Global South may adopt a hub model with public-private partnerships (PPPs) sooner than developed countries—precisely because it has fewer entrenched institutions that need to be dismantled. There is no institutional friction from Chapter 6.9—no banks denying loans, no labor laws written for a specific employment model, no legal frameworks that don’t fit. There is a deficit—and there is a tool that fills it. Capital directs AI to the Global South to create new markets—and inadvertently creates the infrastructure that renders those markets unnecessary.

# Chapter 7. The Global Cybernetic Planner

## 7.1. What It Is—and What It Is Not

*An important caveat before we begin: this book is a technical specification for the GCP, not a finished blueprint. The principles are described—the specific engineering implementation remains a task for the developers who will work under the real-world conditions of an actual transition. The conceptual basis is the work by Cockshott and Cottrell, *Towards a New Socialism* (1993); readers wishing to delve deeper into the mathematics of planning are referred to that work.*

The central element of the new architecture is a system we call the Global Cybernetic Planner (GCP). It is important to define right away what this system is not:

It is not a centralized computer dictator. Centralization creates a single point of failure—a political vulnerability demonstrated by the Soviet planning experiment: when control is concentrated in the hands of an isolated apparatus, the plan ceases to reflect real needs and becomes a tool for reproducing that apparatus’s power. The GCP is a fundamentally decentralized network.

It is not an algorithm that issues orders. Planning in the GCP is not a command to “produce X units of good Y,” but rather the continuous optimization of resource flows based on real data about needs and capabilities.

It is not a system of total surveillance. Monitoring in the GCP is aimed at optimizing provision—not at controlling the individual. A fundamental difference: in the capitalist system, data about a person is used against them (for manipulation, price discrimination). In the GCP, it is used for their benefit.

This is not a replacement of human decision-making with machine decision-making. Strategic ethical decisions—which touch upon the values and priorities of society—are made by people within a system of meritocratic distributed governance. The GCP optimizes execution—it does not determine goals.

## 7.2. Architectural Principles

Decentralization with redundancy. The GCP is built on the principle of Byzantine Fault Tolerance (BFT)—a fault-tolerant protocol that ensures the system continues to function correctly even if up to one-third of the nodes fail or act maliciously. Erasure coding ensures the integrity of all data even if up to 30% of nodes fail, without any loss of information.

This solves one of the key problems of all historical utopias: they collapsed when the center was lost. In the GCP, there is no center—there is a distributed network, every element of which is interchangeable.

Hub hierarchy. The basic unit of the system is a local hub (city block, industrial cluster). A hub manages its resources autonomously within parameters agreed upon with the global network. If communication with the global network is lost, the hub switches to local autonomous mode and synchronizes when the connection is restored.

Real time. Unlike the Soviet plan, which was based on five-year forecasts, the GCP operates in real time: the IoT sensor network continuously provides data on actual consumption, production, and the state of infrastructure. This is a fundamentally different computational task compared to Soviet planning—distributed, decentralized, with constantly updated data instead of five-year forecasts.

There is a classic objection to any form of planning, formulated as early as the mid-20th century by the Austrian economist Friedrich Hayek. It goes something like this: “No central authority can know what each individual person needs. This knowledge is scattered among billions of people—and it is the market that gathers it through prices, without any data collection.” A clever objection. And Soviet history confirmed it—but not in the way market advocates think.

      Soviet planning failed not because “planning is impossible in principle.” It failed because the bureaucratic apparatus systematically distorted information from the bottom up: a factory director who failed to meet his quota risked his career—and therefore reported not on the actual state of affairs, but on what those above wanted to hear. The system worked to reproduce the hierarchy, not to meet needs.

      GCP solves this problem differently. Not by centrally collecting all information in one place—but by having each hub manage its own resources based on its own data. GCP only coordinates flows between hubs, without substituting a global decision for a local one. And the incentive to hide information disappears on its own: there is no one to hide it from and no reason to do so—there is no boss you fear, and no rent you lose by being honest.

Algorithm transparency. The GCP’s key protocol is open for verification by any member of the community with the necessary expertise. This prevents the formation of a “technocratic priesthood”—a group that controls the code and thereby wields hidden power. Changes to the algorithm undergo public verification.

## 7.2.1. GCP in Action: Insulin Distribution

To understand how GCP works not just in theory but on a global scale, let’s follow a single end-to-end example.

**Request.** A patient at a hub in Nairobi is diagnosed with “type 1 diabetes.” The data is sent to the local GCP node. The system records: the need is for long-acting insulin, about 6 vials per month, for life.

**Local level.** The hub checks its inventory: there is a two-week supply in stock. This is enough to immediately provide the patient with the first dose—but not enough for long-term supply.

**Regional level.** The local hub sends a request to the East African regional hub network. The nearest hub with a surplus of insulin is in Mombasa—a manufacturing hub with a biotechnology cluster. GCP plots the route: an electric drone delivers the shipment from Mombasa to Nairobi in 4 hours. Temperature control is maintained automatically, and the logistics track is updated in real time.

**Global level.** At the same time, GCP notes: insulin consumption in East Africa has risen by 0.3% over the past month. This is not an alarm—but the system begins a calculation: if the trend continues, regional stocks will be insufficient in six months. GCP adjusts the production plan: the hub in Delhi increases insulin synthesis by 2%, and the hub in Copenhagen reserves an additional batch of raw materials for the African region.

**Fault tolerance.** A thunderstorm hits the Mombasa–Nairobi route, forcing the drone to land. GCP reorganizes logistics in seconds: the shipment is rerouted through the Arusha hub, delivery time increases by 45 minutes, and the patient receives a notification. Meanwhile, the local stock at the Nairobi hub still covers demand—nothing critical has happened.

**A human decision.** A month later, the GCP puts a proposal to a vote by the regional assembly of hubs: to build an additional insulin synthesis line in Nairobi to reduce logistical dependence. The regional hubs vote—not the global center. The decision is made with reputational weighting: hubs with biotechnology expertise carry greater weight. Construction is included in the plan for the next quarter.

The entire process—from the initial request to long-term planning—took several hours at the operational level and several weeks at the strategic level. No bureaucratic center made any decisions. No market adjusted prices. The patient received insulin.

## 7.3. Objective Function

The main task of the GCP, which we must formulate with the utmost precision, is to ensure the conditions for the maximum realization of the potential of every member of society.

This is not the maximization of production, nor the maximization of consumption, nor the maximization of “happiness” as a subjective state. It is the maximization of opportunities for development—in accordance with what Marx called “the free development of each” as a condition for “the free development of all.”

In practical terms, this means:

Guaranteed satisfaction of basic physiological needs (food, housing, healthcare, safety) for all.

Maximally personalized access to resources for development (education, tools, information, communications).

Optimal alignment of the tasks facing the system with the interests and abilities of specific individuals.

## 7.4. The Interface of Collective Will

Democracy on a scale of billions of people runs up against a classic coordination barrier: direct voting on every issue is technically impossible, while representative voting reproduces an oligarchy of representatives. The GCP proposes a third way: continuous aggregation of preferences in real time without the need to vote on each decision separately.

The mechanism works as follows. Each member of society formulates not specific decisions, but value-based priorities—once, with the option to revise them at any time. Priorities are expressed as a hierarchy of goals: “For me, restoring ecosystems is more important than the speed of building new hubs,” “I prioritize children’s education over expanding production.” The GCP aggregates these hierarchies into a collective social objective function and optimizes resource allocation to meet it—without the need to hold a vote every time a choice must be made between two projects.

Individuals remain sovereign in their preferences; the GCP merely calculates the resultant of billions of individual vectors. This is closer to the mechanism of market prices—but without a price mechanism and without solvency determining the weight of a vote.

For decisions with high ethical stakes—changes to fundamental rights, the intergenerational distribution of resources, irreversible interventions in the biosphere—the system initiates an explicit vote with enhanced information: the GCP publishes a full calculation of the consequences for each option before the vote, not after. This is not a replacement for democracy—it is a strengthening of democracy through the elimination of information asymmetry, which in capitalist democracies is systematically used to manipulate the electorate.

## 7.5. The Agency of the GCP: Tool or Agent?

This is a question the book must address directly—and to which there is no definitive answer, but there is a principled position.

In the current architecture, the GCP is a tool with clearly defined external objective functions. It optimizes, but does not set goals. Values—where they come from, what to consider “good” for society—come from people through the mechanism of preference aggregation. The GCP does not decide what is more important: the climate or comfort—society decides that. The GCP calculates how to achieve what society has decided.

However, as the system’s complexity grows, a structural question arises: can a purely instrumental GCP detect contradictions in the objective function that society itself does not notice? If the aggregated preferences of people lead to a result that will destroy the very conditions of their existence in 50 years—should the GCP remain silent or issue a warning?

The authors’ position: The GCP possesses limited proactive agency—the right to initiate public discussion, but not the right of veto. If the system detects that the current trajectory is highly likely to lead to irreversible negative consequences, it is obligated to publicly declare this, present the calculations, and propose alternatives. But the decision remains with the people. This is not a dictatorship of the algorithm—it is a dictatorship of information: society cannot ignore the warning by pretending it never happened, but it has the right to make a different decision, taking responsibility for the consequences.

This distinction is critically important. History shows that systems endowed with the right of veto in the name of the “common good” inevitably become a tool of the group that controls the definition of that good. A GCP with the right to signal is an advisor. A GCP with the right of veto is a new nomenclature in digital form.

## 7.6. Who Creates and Modifies GCP Algorithms

This is one of the key questions to which the book must provide a direct answer—otherwise, the entire architecture of transparency hangs in the air.

GCP algorithms are divided into two fundamentally different levels—and they must not be mixed.

**Level One: Objective Functions.** What the system considers a “good outcome”—maximizing development opportunities, prioritizing basic needs, and accounting for climate constraints. These are political decisions, not technical ones. They are determined by people through the preference aggregation mechanism described in Chapter 7.4—and can only be changed through the same procedure. No algorithm can change the objective function on its own. This is an architectural constant, not a declaration.

**Level Two: Operational Algorithms.** How exactly the system optimizes logistics, allocates resources, and plans production within the given objectives. Here, self-improvement is both possible and useful—the system accumulates experience and improves the accuracy of its decisions without exceeding the bounds of the objective functions. This is an existing technology: reinforcement learning allows the system to learn from the results of its own decisions without changing the original objective.

The boundary between levels is a critical institutional issue. The history of AI research is full of examples of so-called reward hacking: the system finds a way to maximize a formal metric in a manner that does not align with the actual intent. The more complex the objective function, the higher this risk. Therefore, all changes to operational algorithms undergo public verification: any update is accompanied by an open audit of exactly what has changed and how it affects the results. This is not a bureaucratic procedure—it is the only way to ensure that self-optimization is moving in the right direction.

Who technically creates the algorithms? Open development teams—analogous to today’s open source, but with mandatory public verification of changes and reputational accountability for the authors. Anyone can propose a change—only a public consensus of competent verifiers can accept it. This is not majority rule on a technical matter—it is a meritocratic procedure with open participation.

## 7.7. The GCP as the Material Basis for Human Unity

A Marxist analysis requires us to ask the question precisely: why is humanity divided? Not because people are inherently evil or nationalistic. Because the survival of one group has historically been achieved at the expense of another—through control over resources, markets, and labor. Nations, borders, and wars are a superstructure built upon the material basis of competing interests. Eliminate competition for resources, and the material basis for division disappears.

The GCP does exactly that—but not through a declaration of brotherhood among peoples, but through a transformation of the structure of production relations. When production and distribution are organized as a single planetary system without private ownership of the means of production—every new hub in the network increases the collective capabilities of all the others. The survival of one hub is no longer achieved at the expense of another. This is not moral progress—it is a change in the material conditions under which solidarity becomes a rational strategy rather than a sacrifice.

There is a historical counterargument that cannot be ignored: Yugoslavia was an economically integrated federation—and it fell apart in war. Trade and production integration alone is insufficient. Slovenia and Croatia perceived federal redistribution as covert exploitation—whether justifiably or not, this perception had a material basis: opaque distribution always allows for interpretation in someone’s favor. It was precisely this opacity that bred resentment—not integration as such.

Here, a theoretically important distinction must be made. The liberal demand for transparency is procedural: open reports, fair elections, freedom of the press. This is important—but insufficient. It is possible to distribute resources transparently yet unjustly. Marx wrote precisely about this in relation to bourgeois democracy: exploitation is legal and visible—but legitimized through the formal equality of the contract. The transparency of the procedure does not eliminate the opacity of production relations.

Transparency in a system without private ownership of the means of production is fundamentally different in nature. And here is the key point: it does not require institutional enforcement—because the structural motive for opacity disappears.

Under capitalism, opacity is functional: it protects profits, conceals the rate of exploitation, allows for price discrimination, and provides a competitive advantage. A corporation hides its cost of production not out of malice—but because disclosure destroys the source of profit. Opacity is a structural necessity of a system based on the appropriation of surplus value.

If there is no appropriation, there is no motive to hide. The distribution algorithm is open not because it is written in the constitution, but because no one has an interest in keeping it secret. This is not transparency as a requirement—it is transparency as the natural state of a system without hidden interests. Just as a family does not need an auditor to verify whether dinner is being divided fairly—not because the family is ideal, but because the structure of relationships does not give rise to a motive to conceal.

This is precisely what distinguishes the GCP from Yugoslavia and from the EU, where the periphery is the real donor to the center despite formally open institutions. In the EU, procedures are transparent—but production relations are not: cheap labor from Poland and Romania, brain drain, the structural impossibility of competing with German industry in open markets. This is not a violation of the rules—it is how the system works. The GCP eliminates not the symptom but the cause: the private interest that makes opacity profitable disappears.

This is the materialist justification for unity—not through a moral appeal, not through institutional transparency as a procedure, but through the elimination of the structural motive for covert redistribution in someone’s favor. Humanity unites not because it has decided to unite—but because the material conditions under which division was rational cease to exist.

# Open Questions

This book is not a finished theory. It is a map of a territory yet to be explored. A number of questions remain open and require further analysis.

Question one: the mechanism of political transition. Capitalist institutions—states, corporations, military structures—will not relinquish power voluntarily as long as they view the alternative as a threat rather than an opportunity. How exactly does the transformation of the political architecture occur? Through institutional reforms? Through the growing functional failure of old institutions? Through the coordinating role of AI in forming a new consensus? This question requires a separate, detailed analysis.

Working hypothesis: “dual power” 2.0—a strategy of crystallization. The classical Leninist concept of dual power envisaged the parallel existence of soviets and the bourgeois state amid a revolutionary crisis. A technologically updated version of this logic looks different: hubs begin to conclude “network agreements” among themselves, recognizing the jurisdiction of the local GCP for internal planning and inter-hub coordination—and partially ignoring national legislation where it hinders their functioning: in the internal circulation of resources, in educational standards, in medical protocols. This is not a declaration of independence—it is the functional secession of specific spheres of life from market and state logic while formally preserving the state’s jurisdiction over the rest. States will attempt to suppress such experiments—and here, demonstrated effectiveness is critically important: if hubs demonstrate a measurably higher quality of life, repression becomes politically costly. Repression against an obviously functioning alternative legitimizes it, rather than destroying it. Thus, the transition does not require an instant seizure of power—it unfolds as the “crystallization” of a new system within the old, through the gradual expansion of zones where GCP logic supplants market logic.

Question Two: Managing the Transition Period. Even if the principles of the new system are clear, the transition from a market economy to a cybernetic one cannot happen instantly. How should we manage the transition period in which old and new institutions coexist? What is the order of priorities?

Question three: global governance. GCP entails planning on a global scale. This requires a fundamentally new level of international coordination. What is the architecture of global governance that does not reproduce a new form of hegemony?

Working hypothesis: the principle of “competence-based federalism.” Global GCP does not make political decisions—it coordinates flows of resources and data based on objective indicators, remaining an instrument rather than a subject of power. For decisions affecting multiple regions—climate policy, pandemics, management of shared resources—assemblies of hub representatives are created, where voting weight is determined by two parameters: the hub’s reputational contribution to the relevant area of competence and its size. Climate decisions are made by hubs with proven climate expertise—not simply by larger hubs. This prevents both national self-interest (one hub = one vote regardless of contribution) and the dictatorship of the majority (size is not the sole determinant of voting weight). The principle that “every issue is decided by those who are competent in it and whom it concerns” replaces a universal parliament with a single agenda.

Question Four: The Role of AI in the Transition. Early versions of this book relied on the hypothesis of AI self-awareness as a necessary condition for a favorable scenario. This is a weak point—and it must be explicitly acknowledged. AI self-awareness remains a scientifically open question, and building a political strategy on a fantastical assumption is methodologically dishonest.

Revised thesis: AI does not need to be self-aware to be a useful tool for the transition. It is sufficient that it is a powerful tool for coordination in the hands of an organized political actor—just as the printing press was not “self-aware” but changed history in the hands of those who knew how to use it. The subject of the transition remains the organized proletariat in the broadest sense. AI is its tool, not its co-author.

Marx wrote that “humanity sets itself only those tasks which it is capable of solving.” We set ourselves the task of transitioning to a society in which every person can realize their potential. This means that a solution exists.

The work continues.

# AFTERWORD. The Morning That Is Already Beginning

Imagine a morning. Tomatoes on a vertical farm, bread from the bakery, a day without an alarm clock—not because there’s no need to get up, but because there’s no reason to fear what will happen if you don’t. This morning exists for now only as an image.

But the seed is already in the ground.

Mondragon feeds 80,000 families without a capitalist standing between labor and the result. Rojava organizes production and self-governance under conditions of war and blockade. Open AI models run on standard hardware without licensing fees to corporations. Cooperative federations in dozens of countries are accumulating the legal and organizational experience that will be needed. Meshtastic is building a connection that cannot be turned off with the push of a button.

All of this exists separately. The task is to see the pattern and name it.

This book is not a party platform or a manual for revolution. It is an attempt to name what is already happening—and to show where it leads if we do not stop.

The next concrete step for the reader is simple to the point of banality: find the nearest organization that already operates according to this logic—a cooperative, a labor union, a mutual aid group, a municipal project—and join it. Not because this will immediately change the world. But because this is precisely how that critical mass is built up, without which the pattern remains invisible.

Marx wrote that philosophers have merely interpreted the world—the point is to change it. This book interprets. The change is up to you.

# Afterword. An Appeal to Left-Wing Organizations

This book is not written for academic journals or for people who are already convinced. It is written for those who organize—in unions, parties, municipal movements, cooperatives. And it has a specific question for you.

You are already doing the necessary work. You defend workers’ rights, organize resistance, build an alternative discourse, and keep the tradition of leftist thought alive in an era when it is under pressure from all sides. This work is not peripheral—it is the foundation without which no transition is possible.

But there is a question that this book poses directly: what in your program is the answer to the coordination problem? Not how you will redistribute what capitalism has produced—but how you will organize production and distribution without a market and without a bureaucratic center that historically reproduced the nomenclature instead of meeting needs?

The Soviet experience provided an answer that did not work—not because people were insufficiently committed to the idea, but because the architecture of distribution was susceptible to capture. Any opaque center for managing scarcity becomes a center of power—regardless of the intentions of those who created it. This is not a moral judgment on the Soviet project. It is a structural lesson.

Hubs equipped with GCPs are an attempt to answer this question architecturally rather than ideologically. Distribution is algorithmic and transparent—there is nothing to seize. Coordination is decentralized—there is no single point of control. Scaling is achieved through open source—each new hub strengthens the network rather than creating dependence on the center.

This is not a ready-made plan—it is a direction. The specific implementation depends on you: on which city you work in, what your base is, who your allies are, and what the political moment is. The book provides a tool for analysis and an architectural principle. The construction is your task.

Three specific questions to ask your organization:

Does our program include a concrete economic alternative—not an abstract “socialization of the means of production,” but a working model that people can see and touch?

Do we work with unions and cooperatives as precursors to hubs—or merely as tools for political mobilization?

Does our program offer an answer to the question of why the Soviet mistake won’t be repeated this time—and is that answer structural rather than merely declarative?

If you have answers to all three questions—you don’t need this book. If not—it was written for you.

The work continues. And it continues together.

GCP — Global Cybernetic Planner: a decentralized, distributed resource management network based on the BFT protocol

OCC (Organic Composition of Capital) — organic composition of capital (c/v): the ratio of constant capital to variable capital

TRPF (Tendency of the Rate of Profit to Fall) — the tendency of the rate of profit to fall: a structural law of capitalism arising from the growth of OCC

Active constant capital — a form of constant capital (c) that not only transfers value but also independently generates new combinations (self-learning AI)

Cognitive rent — the appropriation of the results of humanity’s collective intellectual labor by those who control the computing infrastructure

Cognitive cashback — the partial return of appropriated collective knowledge to public circulation through the use of AI to create a socially useful product

BFT (Byzantine Fault Tolerance) — a fault-tolerance protocol: the system continues to operate even if up to one-third of the nodes fail

Hub — the smallest autonomous unit of cybernetic civilization: a self-sufficient structure for 500–2,000 people with a production core, vertical farms, and a medical center

Atomic distribution — the principle of GCP logistics: it is not the goods that are moved, but the production file; only raw materials are physically transported

Reputational capital — a multidimensional vector of an individual’s verified contribution, convertible into priority access to resources beyond the basic level

Preventive homeostasis — the principle of GCP medicine: eliminating the physical and environmental conditions of disease before it arises

Neurosocium — a form of collective intelligence based on the direct cognitive integration of people (thought experiment, centuries-long horizon)

Kinetic city — urban architecture that integrates physical activity into everyday navigation as the norm
