# Chapter 0. Day

Imagine a summer morning.

A large window, and beyond it—the green wall of the neighboring building, already bathed in sunlight. Tomatoes on the upper tiers, greens below, something with a pungent scent in the rain right at the base. Silence—not dead, but alive: voices somewhere far away, a bird, the sound of a bicycle on the lower level.

You wake up not because of an alarm clock. But from an excess of energy and curiosity. What will the new day bring?

This isn’t a metaphor or an advertising slogan. It’s the physiology of a person who has no reason not to sleep well—because yesterday he did what interested him, went to bed when he wanted, and tomorrow holds nothing he fears.

You head down to the floor’s kitchen. The bread from the bakery downstairs is still warm. Cheese, fruit, coffee. All of this is simply there—not because you paid for it, but because that’s how the hub works. Food is produced here, distributed here, and the only decision you need to make in the morning is whether to take a tomato or not.

It’s cool outside. The hub is waking up gradually. The bike paths are already bustling—people are riding somewhere to run their errands, not according to their employer’s schedule, but out of their own inner need. You walk through the garden—dew on the leaves, the smell of earth—and think about the task you took on yesterday. Not because you have to. Because it won’t let you go.

That’s the difference.

It’s not that the difficulties have disappeared—they haven’t gone anywhere. Tasks can be complex, people can be unbearable, and things might not work out for weeks. But the difficulties now are yours. Not imposed by a system that demands your time, but chosen by you because they’re important or interesting, or because there’s no one else to do them.

During the day, you work. “Work” isn’t quite the right word. You do what you’re good at and what draws you in. It could be anything: design, farming, teaching, research, construction, caring for people, creating beauty. The system knows what you’re capable of and what you need—and offers tasks where these intersect. You choose.

Your contribution is recognized—not by a boss, but by the network itself. Not through money, but through reputation: a living, multidimensional, honest one. A job well done opens new opportunities. A job poorly done closes them. This isn’t punishment; it’s simply feedback from reality.

In the evening, you return. In the hub’s atrium, someone is playing—live music, just because they feel like it. Children are running around the lower level. Elderly people are sitting by the fountain and chatting. You know half the people around you by name—not because you have to, but because you live here and these are your people.

You go to bed with the feeling that the day was real.

It’s not like this for everyone every day. Not right away, and not everywhere. But this isn’t a utopia or a fantasy—it’s the logical consequence of a simple solution: organizing production and distribution in the interests of people, not in the interests of capital. How exactly this works, why it’s possible, and what path leads there—that’s what this book is about.
