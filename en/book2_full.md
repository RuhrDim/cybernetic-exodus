# Chapter 0. Day

Imagine a summer morning.

A large window, and beyond it—the green wall of the neighboring building, already bathed in sunlight. Tomatoes on the upper tiers, greens below, something with a pungent scent in the rain right at the base. Silence—not dead, but alive: voices somewhere far away, a bird, the sound of a bicycle on the lower level.

You wake up not because of an alarm clock. But from an excess of energy and curiosity. What will the new day bring?

This isn’t a metaphor or an advertising slogan. It’s the physiology of a person who has no reason not to sleep well—because yesterday he did what interested him, went to bed when he wanted, and tomorrow holds nothing he fears.

You head down to the floor’s kitchen. The bread from the bakery downstairs is still warm. Cheese, fruit, coffee. All of this is simply there—not because you paid for it, but because that’s how the hub works. Food is produced here, distributed here, and the only decision you need to make in the morning is whether to take a tomato or not.

It’s cool outside. The hub is waking up gradually. The bike paths are already bustling—people are riding somewhere to run their errands, not according to their employer’s schedule, but out of their own inner need. You walk through the garden—dew on the leaves, the smell of earth—and think about the task you took on yesterday. Not because you have to. Because it won’t let you go.

That’s the difference.

It’s not that the difficulties have disappeared—they haven’t gone anywhere. Tasks can be complex, people can be unbearable, and things might not work out for weeks. But the difficulties now are yours. Not imposed by a system that demands your time, but chosen by you because they’re important or interesting, or because there’s no one else to do them.

During the day, you work. “Work” isn’t quite the right word. You do what you’re good at and what draws you in. It could be anything: design, farming, teaching, research, construction, caring for people, creating beauty. The system knows what you’re capable of and what you need—and offers tasks where these intersect. You choose.

Your contribution is recognized—not by a boss, but by the network itself. Not through money, but through reputation: a living, multidimensional, honest one. A job well done opens new opportunities. A job poorly done closes them. This isn’t punishment; it’s simply feedback from reality.

In the evening, you return. In the hub’s atrium, someone is playing—live music, just because they feel like it. Children are running around the lower level. Elderly people are sitting by the fountain and chatting. You know half the people around you by name—not because you have to, but because you live here and these are your people.

You go to bed with the feeling that the day was real.

It’s not like this for everyone every day. Not right away, and not everywhere. But this isn’t a utopia or a fantasy—it’s the logical consequence of a simple solution: organizing production and distribution in the interests of people, not in the interests of capital. How exactly this works, why it’s possible, and what path leads there—that’s what this book is about.

# Chapter 8. The Sovereignty of Purpose and the Architecture of Unlocking Potential

Before describing the mechanisms of the GCP, we must answer the question that Marxist analysis poses first: who is the subject of the system, and in whose interests does it function? Without a clear answer to this question, any architecture—regardless of its technical elegance—risks reproducing the old contradiction in a new form: relations of production that serve the interests of the ruling class rather than those of society as a whole.

# 8.1. Sovereignty and the Objective Function

A simple question: who decides what the economy works toward? Formally—everyone. Democratic elections, the constitution, citizens’ rights. In practice—those who control production. Because the state is structurally dependent on them: if business leaves, investment falls, unemployment rises, and tax revenue shrinks. The government may want to reduce emissions—but cannot afford to shut down the oil industry. It may want to raise taxes on the wealthy—but cannot risk capital flight. The system defines the limits of what is possible before voters have a chance to decide anything.

      This is what Marx called the contradiction between the declared and the actual objective function: the system says one thing, does another—not because politicians are bad people, but because the system’s architecture leaves them no choice.

A cybernetic system resolves this contradiction not through declarations, but through architecture. The sovereign of the system is the majority of its members—and this is enshrined not in a constitutional text, but in the very structure of decision-making: algorithms, experts, and institutions are not sources of values. They are tools for realizing a goal that society itself affirms and has the right to revise.

The highest goal is formulated in the same way Marx formulated it in the “Manifesto”: the free development of each as a condition for the free development of all. In operational terms of the GCP, this means maximizing the realization of every member of society’s potential while maintaining the stability of the system itself. Stability here is not an end in itself nor a conservative brake, but a necessary condition: a system that depletes its resources for the sake of a short-term maximum destroys the very possibility of further development. This is a dialectical unity, not a contradiction.

# 8.2. Equality of Opportunity, Not of Outcome

Liberal criticism of left-wing projects traditionally boils down to a single thesis: equalizing outcomes stifles incentives for development and breeds mediocrity. This thesis is true—but it is directed against a straw man. A cybernetic system does not strive for equality of outcomes. People’s abilities, interests, temperaments, and psychological traits vary—this is a biological fact, not social injustice.

The system’s task is different: to eliminate inequality of opportunity. Capitalism reproduces class differences primarily through unequal access to developmental resources in childhood and adolescence—nutrition, environment, education, healthcare, and safety. A child born into a well-off family receives a neurophysiological foundation (Chapter 14) that a child living in conditions of chronic stress and poverty is deprived of through no fault of their own. This is not a competition of abilities—it is a competition of starting conditions, disguised as a competition of merit.

The GCP eliminates this structurally: equal access to education, healthcare, nutrition, and tools for cognitive development is ensured not as charity, but as a systemic priority. Inherited advantages in access to public resources are architecturally excluded—reputational capital is not inherited and requires constant confirmation through current contributions (Chapter 11). Inequality in outcomes is not only permissible—it is inevitable and a sign of a healthy system: the diversity of developmental trajectories is a condition for its sustainability and a source of innovation.

An unresolved issue: invisible inheritance. Equal formal access to resources does not eliminate one structural advantage: the children of people with high reputational contributions grow up in a more intellectually and socially stimulating environment. If your parents are scientists or engineers, you hear conversations about problems and research at dinner, and you absorb their way of thinking and their very attitude toward knowledge—before school even begins. No algorithm can measure this. But it is a real head start—and it cannot be eliminated by access architecture alone.

Working hypothesis: the mechanism of compensatory pedagogy. By analyzing a child’s cognitive profile over time, the system can suggest—not impose—additional educational resources, mentorship, or participation in projects that compensate for a potential deficit in the family’s cultural capital. Modality is of fundamental importance: the system generates a proposal, the acceptance of which is the sovereign choice of the child and the family. This is not a correction of “lagging behind” (lagging behind is determined relative to whose norm?)—it is an expansion of the horizon of possibilities where it is situationally narrowed. The mechanism is embedded in the principle of an equal start not as an exception, but as its operational component.

# 8.3. What Is Potential

The concept of “potential” in the context of this system requires a precise definition—otherwise, it easily becomes a tool for exerting pressure. Potential is not the maximum of cognitive productivity, nor is it the ideal result on a standardized aptitude test. It is the totality of a specific person’s capabilities: intellectual, emotional, physical, creative, and social—in their interaction and dynamics.

Neurobiology confirms that potential is not a fixed quantity. It expands or contracts depending on the environment, experience, and personal choice. This is precisely why the system’s task is not to “unlock potential” as the execution of some predetermined program, but to provide conditions in which a person can do so themselves, at their own pace and along their own path.

This includes the right to stop. A person’s conscious choice not to strive for further growth in any particular area is neither a pathology nor a renunciation of the social contract. It is a sovereign decision. The system does not compel maximum productivity: it provides the opportunity for growth, but does not impose it. Compulsion toward development is alienation in a new form, and it contradicts the very purpose of the system.

# 8.4. Self-correction as an institution

Any system that claims to be rational must incorporate a mechanism for self-correction. Otherwise, it reproduces the main flaw of Soviet planning—the inability to admit mistakes.

      Over time, a distinct class of administrators emerged in the USSR—the nomenklatura: party officials, factory directors, and department heads. Their financial well-being depended directly on whether they retained their position in the hierarchy. And a position was retained only if the plan was fulfilled—or at least appeared to be fulfilled. Therefore, every mistake, every failure was concealed, falsified, or rebranded as a success. The system was physically incapable of learning from its mistakes—because admitting mistakes threatened those who controlled it.

In the GCP, self-correction is part of normal functioning, not an emergency procedure. If a decision adopted by the majority is predicted to hinder the achievement of the goal, the system publishes calculations and reasoning, proposes alternatives, and, if necessary, puts changes to the parameters of its own evaluation model to a vote. Society is capable of revising not only decisions but also the methods used to evaluate them—this is a fundamental difference from any dogmatic system, including historical versions of “scientific communism.”

The algorithm, however, does not possess the right of veto. It models consequences, identifies risks, and initiates revisions—but the final decision rests with the majority. Expert reputational weight is taken into account in specialized decisions (Chapter 11), yet the sovereignty of the goal belongs to society, not to the apparatus—whether technocratic or algorithmic.

# 8.5. Resource Allocation Under Conditions of Structural Deficit

The directions of human development are practically infinite, whereas resources—despite their abundance compared to the capitalist system—are finite. This creates a constant task of prioritization that cannot be solved either by the market (which prioritizes effective demand, not potential) or by a bureaucratic center (which prioritizes its own reproduction).

The GCP solves this through a three-tiered logic. The basic priority is the equitable expansion of starting opportunities for all: this is the foundation without which everything else loses its meaning. The second level is support for areas with a multiplier effect: research that opens up new opportunities for thousands of people takes priority over research whose results are self-contained. The third level is investment in high uncertainty: a portion of resources is deliberately directed toward areas where there is no guaranteed result, but where there is potential for a radical expansion of the system’s capabilities. This is an institutionalized bet on unpredictability—what the “right to unpredictability” from Chapter 15 means at the level of systemic resources, not just individual behavior.

# Chapter 9. The Law of the Sensory Limit and the Finiteness of Needs

# 9.1. Deconstructing the Myth of Infinite Needs

“Human needs are infinite”—so it is written in any economics textbook. This is presented as an axiom, almost as a biological fact. In reality, it is an ideological construct created in the interests of a system that requires continuous consumption.

Neurophysiology refutes this thesis from a biological standpoint. A human being is a biological system with a finite capacity of receptors and neural networks.

Physiological Basis. Needs for nutrients, warmth, safety, and medical care have a strict physiological plateau. Once the consumption norm is reached, each subsequent unit of a resource yields diminishing returns—down to zero or even negative (obesity, chronic intoxication).

Sensory barrier. The senses and cognitive information-processing abilities have a finite bandwidth. It is impossible to simultaneously enjoy a hundred dishes or listen to ten symphonies. Additional pairs of shoes beyond a reasonable number do not provide additional pleasure.

Time. The only truly scarce resource is the 24 hours in a day. All needs compete for this finite time resource, making aggregate demand physically limited.

# 9.2. The Advertising “Overlay” on Biological Needs

If basic needs are finite, where does the perception of “endless” needs come from? Answer: from marketing, which creates artificial needs by exploiting psychological vulnerabilities.

Advertising activates the dopamine reward system through the mechanism of social comparison (status-driven consumption), fear of missing out (FOMO), and direct manipulation of neural circuits via visual and auditory triggers. Consumption in this system is not the satisfaction of a real need, but an attempt to drown out the anxiety created by the system itself.

In a society where advertising disappears and status-driven consumption loses its meaning (since the hierarchy of access to resources through money also disappears), consumption returns to biologically grounded levels. Aggregate demand becomes not only finite but also calculable.

Here we must address an objection: aren’t status and symbolic needs infinite by nature, regardless of the economic system? The answer is no—in their current form, they are socially constructed. Status-driven consumption arises from a specific mechanism: in capitalist society, money is converted into access to resources, and conspicuous consumption signals the presence of money. This is not a biological need—it is an adaptation to the structure of incentives. When this structure is eliminated, status competition does not disappear, but changes form: from “who has the more expensive car” to “whose contribution is more significant”—that is, precisely to the reputation economy described in Chapter 11.4. The need for recognition of competence is biologically grounded; the need for an expensive car as a status signal is not. The former remains and becomes the engine of development. The latter dies out along with the system that gave rise to it.

# Chapter 10. Urbanism of the Future: Liberated Space

# 10.1. The City as a Machine for the Car

The modern city is a space where 40–60% is occupied not by people, but by infrastructure for servicing and storing private cars. Roads, interchanges, and above- and below-ground parking lots consume a large portion of the city’s territory. This is not a result of convenience for people—it is a result of the city’s architecture having been optimized to meet the demands of the real estate market and the automotive industry.

According to urban studies (ITDP and OECD), each passenger car in the city requires 2–3 parking spaces, each covering an area of about 12.5 m². Meanwhile, a private car spends 90% of its time parked—taking up space and losing value. With the transition to a decentralized “swarm” of autonomous vehicles (Mobility-as-a-Service), which are constantly on the move and return to compact depots for charging, the required number of vehicles is reduced by 85–90%.

# 10.2. The Math of Freeing Up Space

In a city the size of Moscow or Berlin, the transition to a cybernetically controlled transport swarm frees up:

Up to 25% of the entire urban area previously occupied by parking lots.

An additional 15–20% due to narrower roads (autonomous vehicles travel in tight columns with minimal spacing, which significantly increases the capacity of narrower lanes).

In total, this amounts to tens of thousands of hectares in every major city—an area equivalent to tens of thousands of soccer fields.

# 10.3. What Replaces the Asphalt

The freed-up space is transformed according to the principle of maximizing development potential:

A fractal city. The urban structure breaks down into autonomous hub clusters, within which housing, laboratories, production facilities, gardens, and resource distribution points are within walking distance. The need for daily commutes disappears.

Green reconquista. Former roads and parking lots are transformed into linear parks and green corridors. Increased vegetation reduces the urban environment’s temperature by 3–5°C (heat island effect). Noise pollution drops by 70–80% with the transition to silent electric transport—which in itself is a radical improvement in quality of life: a reduction in chronic stress from noise directly correlates with a decrease in cortisol levels.

Local production. The principle of atomic distribution (Chapter 3.4) is directly implemented in the hub’s architecture: production spaces are equipped with a full range of additive and subtractive equipment—from FDM and resin printing to CNC milling machines and metalworking stations. The GCP stores and distributes production files globally; only raw materials are physically moved. The path from concept to product is reduced to a few hours and a few dozen meters. Vertical farms apply the same logic to food: the “farm-to-table” cycle is closed within the hub, eliminating the cold chain and transportation emissions.

Mobility of concepts, not of things. Housing transforms from an object of ownership into a functional service. The residential module of any hub is equipped with an identical basic set. Moving to participate in a project in another city or country takes hours and does not require transporting personal belongings.

# 10.4. Anatomy of a hub: the minimal autonomous unit

A hub is not just a neighborhood or a residential complex. It is the smallest self-sufficient unit of cybernetic civilization: a structure capable of supporting the full life cycle of its members in an autonomous mode while simultaneously functioning as a node in the global GCP network.

A typical hub is designed for 500–2,000 people—a size at which both personal acquaintance among most members (which activates the oxytocin mechanisms of social bonding) and sufficient specialization to maintain a full range of competencies are possible. This is not an arbitrary figure. Anthropologist Robin Dunbar has shown that the human brain is capable of maintaining stable social bonds with approximately 150 people simultaneously—this is the so-called Dunbar number. The hub is organized according to the principle of nested levels of closeness: the first level consists of those with whom a person works every day and knows personally (~150 people); the second level consists of acquaintances with whom collaboration is possible (~500); the third level consists of all hub members with whom you share a common infrastructure and shared decisions (~2,000). This is not a hierarchy of trust in the sense of “who you trust more”—these are levels of interaction intensity. Just like in any urban neighborhood: you know your neighbor in the stairwell differently than someone from the next street—both are part of the same neighborhood, but the degree of familiarity is different.

The hub’s physical layout is organized around concentric zones of accessibility. The core—comprising public spaces, production facilities, a medical center, and educational laboratories—is within walking distance of any residential area (no more than 5–7 minutes). The second zone—vertical farms, energy infrastructure, and recreational areas—is a 10–15-minute brisk walk away. The outer perimeter opens onto green corridors between the hubs.

Housing is integrated into the hub’s structure—it is not located outside its boundaries as a separate residential area. Residential blocks are located within walking distance of the core: these can be apartment buildings, sections with shared kitchens and workspaces, or compact individual modules—the specific form is determined by the climate, traditions, and the hub’s own design. The principle is simple: from your bed to the fablab, medical center, and public space—on foot, without transportation.

The hub’s production core includes: a full-cycle fab lab (FDM, resin and metal printing, laser cutting, CNC milling), an entry-level biolab, and workshops for working with wood, textiles, and electronics. This is not a craft museum—it is a real production infrastructure that covers 60–70% of the hub’s needs for goods through local production based on files from the GCP’s global library.

Food security is based on local production adapted to the climate and geography. In temperate climates—vertical hydroponic farms. Here, a fair disclaimer is necessary: vertical farms yield 10–20 times more per square meter than traditional farms, require no pesticides, and are not dependent on the weather—but they consume significantly more electricity for lighting. When powered by renewable sources, the energy balance is positive; with coal-fired generation, it is negative. This means that vertical farms, as the foundation of a hub’s food autonomy, only work in conjunction with a transition to clean energy—they are not a standalone solution, but part of a system. In the tropics—year-round open vegetable gardens and orchards. Beyond the Arctic Circle—heated greenhouses with supplemental lighting and storage. The technology is secondary—the principle is one: maximum food autonomy achievable under the given conditions. The remainder comes from the regional network of hubs via GCP logistics. Kitchens in the hub are communal and professional—not because private cooking is prohibited, but because collective food preparation using shared resources is structurally more efficient and socially richer.

The hub’s energy system is distributed and redundant. The specific mix depends on geography: solar panels in southern latitudes, geothermal energy in volcanic zones, wind on the coasts and in the steppes, and combinations in temperate climates. There is one constant: energy storage and connection to the regional grid ensure autonomy regardless of the primary source. The BFT principle applies not only to data but also to physical infrastructure: the hub is designed with a guaranteed reserve of autonomy—if external supplies are cut off, basic services continue to operate without degradation.

The hub’s medical center is not a polyclinic in the Soviet sense. It is a space for continuous monitoring and preventive intervention (Chapter 14.8): diagnostic equipment, physical therapy zones, and psychological support offices. Acute cases are routed to specialized inter-hub centers via GCP transport. The principle: 80% of medical needs are met within the hub, while 20% require external specialization.

Hub management is decentralized. Operational decisions (scheduling of production equipment, maintenance, resource logistics) are optimized based on data from the sensor network—the algorithm is transparent and verifiable by any hub member. Strategic decisions (new projects, changes to spatial architecture, development priorities) are put to a vote by hub members with reputation weighting based on their profile (Chapter 11.4). Connection to the global GCP network ensures coordination between hubs without a centralized governing body.

# Chapter 11. The Biology of Motivation: The Economics of Meaning

# 11.1. The Failure of Coercion as a Motivator

The question of labor motivation in a money-free society is most often posed as an appeal to “human nature”: it is claimed that without the fear of starvation or the desire for enrichment, people will cease to work. This claim does not stand up to scientific evidence.

Coercion (fear of punishment) is the least effective motivator for complex cognitive activity. This is well documented in the psychology of work: external motivators stimulate mechanical labor but suppress creative activity—an effect known as “crowding out.”

# 11.2. Evolutionary Drivers

Evolutionary psychology offers a different picture of human motivation—one less convenient for the apologists of the lazy person, but significantly more optimistic for a society that has rid itself of coercion.

Status and competence. Humans are deeply social primates. Evolution has established a powerful reward system for acquiring competence and having that competence recognized by the group. Mastering a complex skill and demonstrating it to one’s peers activates the serotonin system—one of the fundamental neurobiological mechanisms of well-being. The thirst for mastery and recognition is not “lofty idealism”; it is a biological necessity.

The state of flow. Psychologist Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi studied the conditions under which people work best and feel most alive. The answer turned out to be precise: this happens when the task is slightly more complex than the current level—difficult enough to avoid boredom, yet manageable enough to avoid panic. He called this state “flow.” In flow, people describe a sense of deep satisfaction and meaning—regardless of whether they are paid for it or not.

Prosociality. Psychologist Michael Tomasello studied young children—before school and upbringing had a chance to instill anything. His conclusion: children instinctively strive to help adults and other children. Not for a reward—just because. The market system suppresses this quality: when people start getting paid for help, intrinsic motivation is extinguished. Why help just for the sake of it if “help” has become a job? Reciprocal altruism is an evolutionarily ancient and powerful strategy that capitalism does not create, but destroys.

Curiosity. The brain is “wired” for problem-solving: that is its evolutionary function. A prolonged absence of meaningful activity leads to anhedonia and the atrophy of neural connections. “Laziness” in its clinical sense is a symptom of alienation and a loss of meaning, not the default state of a healthy person.

# 11.3. The Dopamine Economy vs. The Economy of Meaning

Capitalism exploits the dopamine system: goods and services are designed to create a short-term surge of dopamine, followed by habituation and the need for a new dose of stimulation. This is an economically efficient system—for producers. For consumers, it creates chronic anxiety, a sense of emptiness, and growing dissatisfaction.

The GCP system shifts the motivational architecture to different neurobiological circuits:

Level | Biological Driver | Implementation Mechanism

I. Mastery | Neuroplasticity, curiosity | Access to the hub’s unique equipment and learning resources proportional to the level of competence

II. Recognition | Serotonin hierarchy | Reputational status within the global knowledge network

III. Belonging | Oxytocin bond | Participation in large-scale, meaningful projects

# 11.4. Reputation System

In the absence of money, reputation becomes the primary economic mechanism—a dynamic indicator of an individual’s contribution to reducing the system’s entropy.

Reputation is not just points in a database. It is a measure of social trust that translates into access to resources. A person with a high reputation in biology has priority access to computing power for their research. A person with a high reputation in pedagogy has greater influence in decisions regarding the hub’s educational programs. This is meritocracy, digitized through the GCP.

It is fundamentally important: reputation is not accumulated passively. It requires constant input. This prevents the formation of inherited status positions—one of the basic mechanisms for reproducing class differences.

The fractal architecture of reputation. The most common criticism of reputation systems—their resemblance to China’s “social credit system”—implies a single numerical scale that sums up all of a person’s behavior into a single number. This is precisely what reputation in the GCP is not.

Reputation in the system is multi-vector: it is not a single index, but a profile of competencies—separate for each field of activity. A high reputation score in quantum physics offers no advantages when voting on urban planning issues. An outstanding engineer does not automatically influence the priorities of medical research. This is not an arbitrary decision by the system’s architects—it is a fundamental safeguard against the emergence of a new technocratic elite whose dominance in one field would translate into power over all others.

Reputation profiles are intentionally not aggregated into a single rating. The GCP stores and uses them as multidimensional vectors: when making a decision in a specific field, only the relevant dimension is taken into account. A person may be a novice in engineering and a master in pedagogy—and receive recognition precisely where it is deserved. This eliminates the monopoly on status and fulfills the biologically grounded need for recognition of competence (Chapter 11.2) without creating a hierarchy of domination.

Protection against the tyranny of the majority. Democratic sovereignty of the majority, enshrined in Chapter 8, creates a structural risk that political philosophy has described since the time of Tocqueville: the majority is capable of systematically infringing on the interests of minorities while remaining within the bounds of formally legitimate procedures. The reputation system does not automatically eliminate this risk—it redistributes it.

Architectural safeguards are built on several principles. First, basic rights—access to food, housing, healthcare, education, and a minimum reputational starting point—are removed from the voting process entirely. The majority cannot vote to deprive a minority of basic conditions for existence: this is not a matter for democratic decision-making, but an architectural constant of the system. Second, decisions affecting the rights of specific groups require a qualified majority, with mandatory inclusion in the voting pool of representatives from the affected group with increased weight. Third, the GCP continuously models the distributional effects of adopted decisions and publicly signals if the implementation of a decision systematically worsens the situation of any subgroup. This does not block the decision—but makes its consequences visible before implementation.

Operational Logic: How Reputation Translates into Access. A person’s reputation profile is a multidimensional vector, each dimension of which reflects a verified contribution in a specific field. Contributions are recorded through three channels: verified results (a completed project, a published study, a trained student—all with measurable outcomes), expert evaluation (assessment by individuals with higher reputational weight in the same field), and long-term impact (whether the result continues to be effective over time—research that is utilized, technology that works).

The conversion of reputation into access to resources follows a priority queue system rather than a market mechanism. If multiple researchers apply for the hub’s supercomputer simultaneously, the GCP establishes a queue based on reputational weight in the relevant field and the project’s predictive value. Those who lose out in the queue receive an alternative offer—access to a similar resource at a neighboring hub or at a different time. The resource is not sold or traded—it is allocated. Scarcity does not create a price; it creates a queue with a transparent algorithm.

Basic access—food, housing, healthcare, education, and a minimum set of tools—is not contingent on reputation. It is guaranteed to everyone regardless of contribution. Reputation unlocks access to resources beyond the basic level: to rare equipment, to participation in priority projects, to expanded computing power. This is not a punishment for those who do not contribute—it is the logic of prioritization in the face of a real shortage of complex resources.

Protection against manipulation. Any system where status is converted into access to resources creates an incentive to inflate that status. In capitalism, this manifests as corruption and cronyism. In a reputation-based system, it would take the form of coordinated groups mutually inflating ratings or fictitious projects.

The GCP protects against this as follows: a reputation rating is considered valid only if it is independently confirmed by multiple sources—this is the same principle of fault tolerance as in the scheduler’s architecture itself. The system does not track personal connections between people—that would be surveillance. It looks for statistical anomalies: if ratings in a certain cluster are systematically above average, and there are no confirmed results—that’s a red flag for verification. Not “these two know each other,” but “these numbers don’t add up.”

Verification of the result is more important than verification of the grade. A study’s reputational weight grows not at the moment of publication, but as other researchers rely on it. A teacher’s reputational weight grows as their students demonstrate measurable progress. This makes long-term falsification structurally unprofitable: a fictitious project does not produce a real effect, meaning the reputation built upon it gradually fades.

Finally—transparency. The reputation calculation algorithm is open to verification by any member of society with sufficient expertise (Chapter 7.2). This prevents a situation in which the algorithm itself becomes a tool of power for those who control it.

Clever manipulation is more difficult to detect than technical glitches: people adapt to algorithms once they understand how they work, hide behind social trust, and act through long, indirect chains. Therefore, the GCP operates on the principle of escalating verification: the higher the stakes, the deeper the analysis. A standard assessment undergoes statistical verification. A major decision with suspicious patterns undergoes cross-analysis from independent sources. Something truly important involves independent experts who did not participate in the decision being reviewed. The threshold between levels is set by the predicted damage from potential manipulation, not by the discretion of specific individuals. In-depth verification is the exception, not the norm. Routine operations proceed without this level of control. There will be no panopticon.

## 11.4.1. How this system breaks down: three attack scenarios

Any system where status is converted into access to resources will be a target for attack. This is not a hypothetical risk—it is an engineering fact.

**Scenario One: Coalition-based inflation.** A group of participants agrees to mutually inflate each other’s reputation scores. If such cross-evaluations originate from a single cluster and are not confirmed by independent sources, the system detects a statistical anomaly: scores within the cluster are systematically higher than those coming from outside. The algorithm reduces the weight of intra-cluster ratings until the discrepancy disappears. Reputation built on mutual inflation begins to fade without external confirmation.

**Scenario Two: Monopoly of the Old Elites.** If a group with high reputational weight dominates a certain field for a long time, there is a risk that newcomers cannot break through not because of a lack of competence but because of a monopoly on expert evaluation. The GCP resolves this through mandatory rotation of expert pools: when evaluating applications, randomly selected experts with moderate reputational weight who are not affiliated with the dominant group must participate. These experts cannot block a decision—but their evaluations influence the final weight.

**Scenario Three: Reputation Inflation.** If reputation only grows and never declines, it eventually loses its distinctiveness. The system applies a decay mechanism: reputation weight that is not confirmed by new contributions within a certain period gradually decreases. This is not a penalty—it reflects the fact that in a rapidly changing environment, past achievements have a limited shelf life. The rate of decay depends on the field: slower in fundamental science, faster in technology.

**A fair disclaimer:** these mechanisms do not guarantee the unhackability of the reputation system. They guarantee that an attack has a predictable cost, that its consequences are limited in time, and that the system is capable of recovering after an anomaly is detected. This is not invulnerability—it is resilience.

# 11.5. Art as a Species-Specific Need

Capitalism treats art as the entertainment industry—a sector of the economy with revenue figures, market share, and a target audience. This is not a random distortion. It is a logical consequence of a system that turns everything within its reach into a commodity. But art existed thirty thousand years before the market emerged—and its nature is not defined by the market.

The cave paintings of Altamira and Lascaux were created approximately 35,000 years ago. That is 25,000 years before agriculture and 30,000 years before money. People created symbolic images in conditions where every calorie was a matter of survival—which means it was important enough to devote resources to. Marx noted this in his “Economic and Philosophical Manuscripts of 1844”: humans produce not only for immediate need, but also according to the laws of beauty. This is not a mere embellishment of life superimposed on the economy—it is a species-defining trait. Homo sapiens is the only species that builds complex symbolic systems—language, myth, narrative—capable of transmitting experience across generations and modeling a reality that does not yet exist.

Neurobiology confirms: the perception of music, painting, and narrative activates the same reward systems as food and social recognition. This is not a metaphor—it is fMRI data. Narrative is the way the brain organizes experience and constructs causal models of the world. Without narrative, there is no understanding. Without aesthetic experience, empathy for others—the ability to model another person’s inner world—does not develop. These are functional needs that ensure the cognitive and social development of the species. Not a luxury.

What does capitalism do with this need? It exploits it. Mass culture is optimized not for the human need for understanding—but for the dopamine response of a solvent audience. It’s the same mechanism that fast food has in relation to nutrition: the system activates a biological mechanism but does not satisfy the underlying need. Art that expands our model of the world, fosters empathy for others’ experiences, and asks uncomfortable questions is not commercially viable. It demands effort from the viewer and offers no guarantee of repeat business. Therefore, it is systematically pushed to the periphery: into state-subsidized institutions with a limited audience, or into the precarious existence of an artist who moonlights as a courier.

In a GCP society, art returns to its function—and fundamentally changes its form of existence.

First: the economic barrier to creation disappears. An artist, musician, or writer does not sell a product—they make a contribution that is evaluated by a reputation system based on its real, long-term impact: whether it has changed the way of understanding of those who have encountered it. Basic needs are met regardless of this evaluation. This is the only historical system where an artist does not have to choose between integrity and survival.

Second: state commissions disappear. In the USSR, art was free from the market—but not free from party ideology. In the GCP, there is no mechanism that makes uncomfortable art economically or politically impossible. The reputation system evaluates the real effect on people—not conformity to an ideological line. This is not enshrined in the constitution as a right—it is a consequence of the architecture.

Third and most importantly: the liberation of time makes creativity a mass practice. When a person doesn’t spend 35 hours a week on necessities, they have time to create. Not everyone will become an artist in the professional sense. But a society where the majority of people have experience in creation—music, text, visual imagery, space—is qualitatively different from a society where only a few create while the rest consume. More people creating means more empathy, more non-trivial connections between fields of knowledge, more questions that a professional won’t ask because they seem obvious to them.

Art in a GCP society is neither an industry nor a pastime. It is the way in which society processes collective experience, expands the horizon of possible understanding, and reproduces empathy as social glue. It is a function without which any system sooner or later loses the ability to see itself from the outside.

# Chapter 12. Social Homeostasis: Without Prisons and Police

# 12.1. The Marxist Diagnosis of Crime

In the Marxist understanding, crime is primarily a product of poverty, inequality, and alienation. If this is true, then eliminating material deprivation and alienated labor should eliminate the root cause of most crimes.

This is confirmed by data from dozens of countries: the level of violent crime correlates more strongly with inequality than with poverty alone. A rich but unequal country is more dangerous than a less wealthy but more equal one. The United States—one of the richest countries in the world—has an incomparably higher level of violence than the Scandinavian countries, with their far more equitable distribution of income. Researchers Richard Wilkinson and Kate Pickett analyzed data from numerous countries and found that the greater the social stratification, the worse everything else is—violence, mental disorders, life expectancy, and levels of trust. Inequality is toxic in and of itself—regardless of the absolute level of prosperity.

Once the material basis of crime is eliminated, a certain residual volume remains: neurodevelopmental pathologies, cognitive distortions, and affective states. This is a matter for medicine, not the penal system.

# 12.2. Deviance as a Medical Marker

From a neurobiological perspective, systematic antisocial behavior is often a symptom of: neurotransmitter imbalances (low serotonin, chronically high cortisol), prefrontal cortex dysfunction (impaired impulse control), and the consequences of early trauma.

None of these causes is addressed by incarceration. Prison isolates the consequence without addressing the cause and, in most cases, exacerbates neurobiological dysfunctions through stress, social deprivation, and exposure to criminal subculture.

The alternative is accessible psychological and medical support without barriers. In a society without time constraints and without fear of losing income, a person in crisis can seek help sooner—not because the system is monitoring them, but because help is genuinely accessible and carries no social stigma. This is a fundamentally different logic: it is not surveillance that prevents a breakdown, but the elimination of the conditions under which a breakdown becomes the only way out.

# 12.3. Conflicts in a World Without Scarcity

Conflicts do not disappear in a world without poverty. They change in nature: shifting from a struggle for material resources to competition for recognition, intellectual priorities, and personal space.

The conflict resolution architecture in the GCP includes several levels:

Meritocratic arbitration. Disputes over resource allocation (for example, two researchers claiming priority access to a supercomputer) are resolved by the GCP based on objective data: the parties’ reputational status and the predictive value of their projects. The losing party automatically receives an alternative proposal.

Mediation. Interpersonal conflicts requiring empathy and an understanding of nuances are referred to mediators—specialists with a high reputation index in the field of social psychology. The GCP ensures impartiality in the selection of mediators.

Reputational sanctions. Destructive behavior leads to a temporary reduction in access privileges. The individual is deprived of the opportunity to participate in management decisions or work with complex equipment—until they complete a course of psychological correction. This is not punishment in the punitive sense, but a restriction of social trust—a direct consequence of one’s own behavior.

# 12.4. Anti-system groups

Even in a perfectly functioning system, there will be people whose beliefs or psychological traits push them toward resistance. This is a sociological inevitability.

Suppressing anti-system movements by force creates martyrs and a spiral of violence. The GCP uses different approaches:

The Right to Withdraw. Groups that fundamentally reject cybernetic control are granted territorial autonomy—the opportunity to live outside the system on designated lands. The GCP ceases active governance of these territories but maintains an “invisible” security perimeter and the option to return. The energy of protest is directed toward building their own way of life, rather than destroying the system.

Information Hyde Park. Special digital spaces for criticizing the system, where freedom of speech is unrestricted. Aggressive propaganda is filtered by reputation algorithms (which reduce reach but do not block the ability to speak out).

Redirecting radical energy. Leaders of anti-system movements, whose motives the GCP analysis interprets as an unfulfilled thirst for significance or leadership, are offered “ultra-complex missions”—tasks where radical energy becomes constructive.

# 12.5. Difficult Cases: An Honest Answer

The Marxist diagnosis of crime as a product of poverty and alienation is statistically accurate—but not absolutely so. There remains a category of cases that cannot be reduced to material conditions: severe violence, sexual violence including child abuse, and murder. These cases exist in all societies, including the most egalitarian ones. An honest theory must address them.

First: the scale will change radically. The overwhelming majority of violence in modern societies is situational: alcohol, drugs, domestic conflict against a backdrop of chronic stress and poverty. This disappears along with the conditions that gave rise to it. What remains—neurobiological pathologies, severe attachment disorders from early childhood, rare cases of psychosis—accounts for a small fraction of the current volume. This does not mean the problem does not exist. It means that its scale allows us to address it on an individual basis, rather than through a system of mass incarceration.

Second: isolation as protection, not punishment. A person who has committed a serious act of violence poses a real threat to others. This threat must be neutralized—but not through punitive incarceration, whose purpose is humiliation and retribution, but through therapeutic isolation: restriction of freedom of movement with full access to medical care, psychotherapy, education, and meaningful activities. The goal is not to punish, but to eliminate the threat and, where possible, restore the ability to live in society. The Scandinavian penitentiary model is already moving in this direction—and shows a radically lower recidivism rate than punitive systems.

Third: irreversible cases. There are people with such damage to their neurobiological architecture—whether congenital or acquired—that recovery is impossible with current medical technology. For them, long-term isolation remains the only solution compatible with public safety. This is an honest acknowledgment of the system’s limits—not its failure.

Fourth: organized crime from the old world. Mafia structures, drug cartels, and criminal hierarchies are not biological pathologies. They are rational adaptations to conditions where the legal market is inaccessible or unjust. They exist because they provide what the system does not: income, protection, and a sense of belonging. In a society where basic needs are met and the reputation economy offers an alternative path to significance—the economic foundation of organized crime disappears. Historical precedent: after the legalization of alcohol in the U.S., the mafia did not disappear instantly—but it lost its main source of income and scale. The transition period will require direct opposition to existing structures—but this is a political task of the transition period, not an architectural problem of the new system.

# Chapter 13. The Biology of Power and Its Channels

# 13.1. The Neurobiology of Domination

The need for control is not a moral vice, but an evolutionary mechanism. But it is important to distinguish between two phenomena that, in everyday language, merge into one.

The first is the need to control one’s environment, one’s process, and the results of one’s labor. This is a healthy need: every person wants to feel that their actions mean something. Psychologists call this the need for agency—it motivates, does not destroy empathy, and lends itself to constructive application.

The second is the need to control other people: hierarchical domination as such. It is precisely this second type that gives rise to systemic pathology.

Modern neuroscience documents this with anatomical precision. Decher Keltner of the University of Berkeley has shown that prolonged domination alters the activation patterns of the prefrontal cortex—the ability to model others’ states diminishes, and a person literally ceases to accurately read what others are feeling. This is not a personality trait—it is a predictable consequence of a prolonged stay in a position of power over others. Susanna Bickert’s research documented structural changes: in people with high social status, the volume of the amygdala—the structure responsible for processing social signals—decreases over time. The brain literally reorganizes itself to fit the new role.

This is called the “paradox of power”: people who gain control over others lose the physiological capacity for empathy—precisely the capacity needed to exercise that control wisely. The system destroys itself. Not because specific people are bad—but because that is the neurobiology of prolonged dominance. It is a biologically predictable consequence, not a moral choice.

But an important clarification is needed here—otherwise the conclusion will be too pessimistic. The paradox of power describes the brain’s adaptation to the specific stimuli that power presents—not to domination as such. Under capitalism, a coordinator works with abstractions: profit figures, performance indicators, growth metrics. People in this system are rows in a table. The brain adapts to working with abstractions and gradually loses the ability to perceive the specific people behind the numbers. This is not malicious intent—it is neuroplasticity responding to real environmental stimuli.

With fundamentally different stimuli, adaptation will be different. A coordinator in the GCP system works with specific tasks for specific people, receives direct feedback from those affected by their decisions, and their reputation score reflects the real human impact—not abstract profit. The brain adapts to what it works with. If stimuli require accurate reading of human states, this ability develops rather than atrophies.

This is confirmed by de Waal’s research: in bonobos, prosocial behavior persists in conditions of abundance and the absence of competition for resources. Dominance in and of itself does not destroy empathy—it is a specific type of dominance linked to competition for scarce resources and the use of abstract metrics of that competition that does. Change the stimuli—and the neurobiology of power changes along with them.

# 13.2. Selfishness as a Product of Scarcity

Egoism is a survival strategy, not a personality trait. Biologist Richard Dawkins has shown that genes are “programmed” to maximize their own propagation—hence competition and aggression. But this does not mean that people are doomed to be egoists. It means that behavior changes along with the environment.

Primatology confirms this with measurable precision: in conditions of abundance and safety, mammals shift toward mutual altruism. Selfishness is a strategy of scarcity. Eliminate scarcity—and the optimal strategy changes on its own.

An important clarification that Marxist analysis adds to the biological one: scarcity in capitalist society is not merely material. A scarcity of recognition, a scarcity of meaning, a scarcity of control over one’s own life—all of these activate the same survival strategies as material need. A person fighting for status in a corporate hierarchy is biologically in the same mode as a person fighting for food. The system creates an artificial scarcity of recognition—and reaps selfishness as a predictable result.

# 13.3. The Architecture of Power Without Power

Control over people as a permanent resource is eliminated not by a declaration of equality—but by specific institutional mechanisms that make its accumulation structurally impossible. Here, the GCP serves as a tool for transparency and accountability, not as a governing entity. Decisions are made by people through open procedures.

Multiple hierarchies of expertise. Instead of a single vertical hierarchy of control over people—a network of areas of competence, each with its own hierarchy based on actual contribution. A person may be a novice in engineering but a master in pedagogy—and gain recognition in their niche. This satisfies the need for agency without creating structures of domination over others.

Rotation of responsibility. Coordinating roles are temporary, not permanent. Leadership on a project is determined by competence and ends with the project. There are no positions—only roles with a specific duration and a specific task. Any hub member can initiate a review of a coordinating role through an open procedure—all coordinators’ decisions are public and verifiable.

Rotation as a way to preserve the full scope of experience. A coordinator who periodically returns to hands-on work sees the system from the inside, not just from above—and brings that experience back to the coordinating role. This is not a correction of a problem or a sign of mistrust. It is a normal cycle of development: like a good teacher who continues to solve problems themselves—not because they would otherwise lose their touch, but because it makes them a better teacher.

This is not a utopian idea—it is a documented practice with measurable results. The Japanese genba system requires managers to regularly work directly on the production floor: Toyota made this a management principle because a manager who does not understand the real process from the inside cannot manage it effectively. In the medical field of many countries, chief physicians are required to maintain clinical practice alongside their administrative duties. In the military, rotation between staff and field duties is mandatory for career advancement.

Historically, the same principle was applied in left-wing self-governance projects. The Paris Commune of 1871—all elected positions with the right of immediate recall and pay no higher than a worker’s wage: the coordinator remains part of the community they coordinate. Yugoslav self-governance institutionalized rotation in enterprise councils. Rojava today—mandatory dual co-presidency and rotation at all levels of governance.

Rotation in the GCP synthesizes both principles: the neurobiological one—periodic returns to direct labor maintain a living understanding of real conditions—and the institutional one—no coordinating role becomes a permanent position. This is not a lack of trust in people—it is an architecture that makes the accumulation of power structurally impossible.

But here we must honestly admit: there is no universal rotation term—and attempting to establish one creates a new problem. A coordinator forced to leave precisely when they have accumulated enough experience to do everything correctly represents a real loss for the system, not an abstract risk.

The solution is differentiation by role type, not a single term for everyone.

Project roles have a natural conclusion: the coordinator leaves when the project is completed—not after an arbitrary period, but upon the resolution of a specific task. The accumulated experience is not lost—it is documented and passed on to the next person. This is the purest mechanism: not personal conflict or arbitrariness, but the logic of the work itself.

Operational roles—hub coordination, infrastructure management—require a fixed term with the possibility of extension. Three to five years is enough to gain experience and not enough for the role to become inseparable from the individual. Renewal is possible—but only through an explicit collective decision by the hub members, not automatically. This shifts the burden of proof: not “prove that you need to leave,” but “prove that you need to stay.”

Research and creative roles—here, mandatory rotation is inherently destructive. Long-term research requires continuity. Three accountability mechanisms operate here—not a fixed term, but constant feedback from those affected by the work.

First—transparency of decisions. All of the coordinator’s decisions are public and accessible to any community member at all times—not an annual report, but an open log: what was decided, based on what data, and what the result was. This isn’t surveillance of a person—it’s the visibility of their decisions. The difference is fundamental.

Second—measurable impact. The reputation system evaluates results, not intentions. A study that no one uses, a project that yields no measurable impact—this is a signal for a collective discussion about the direction of the work. Not a punishment—but a basis for reconsideration.

Third—the right to initiate a review. Any community member can raise the issue of reviewing a role through an open procedure with reputation-weighted voting. This is not a denunciation or intrigue—it is a formalized feedback mechanism.

Here, we must honestly acknowledge the system’s limitations. All three mechanisms work against obvious failures—but they are ineffective against gradual degradation, which is difficult to measure. A researcher who has been working on an irrelevant topic for twenty years—how can this be detected before it becomes obvious? Academic science under capitalism faces the same problem and has not fully resolved it. The GCP does not solve it either—it merely removes the financial incentive that, under capitalism, further distorts the direction of research. This is an improvement—but not a solution to the problem. This is precisely why a culture of open criticism and an institutionalized right to dissent are not an addition to the system but its essential element: where the algorithm fails to detect gradual degradation, people working alongside it can see it and initiate public discussion.

# Chapter 14. The Human Life Cycle in the New Society

# 14.1. Pregnancy and Birth: Support, Not Control

In a cybernetic system, pregnancy is viewed not as a private family matter—but as a process for which society is obligated to create the most favorable conditions possible. Not because the state knows better—but because the expectant mother should have no cause for anxiety that the system could eliminate.

What does this mean in practice? Housing, food, medical care—all of this is provided without bureaucracy, waiting lines, or financial barriers. Not as a privilege, but as the norm. A woman expecting a child should not have to worry about whether she has enough money for a doctor or whether she will have to return to work before her due date.

Prenatal care is continuous and unobtrusive—not for monitoring, but to catch any issues early. Childbirth takes place in a space that combines safety with a human atmosphere—neither a hospital assembly line nor a risky home experiment, but something in between.

The first hours after birth are protected: mother and child together, without separation for procedures that can wait. This isn’t sentimentality—it’s what evidence-based medicine has long recommended and what the capitalist healthcare system systematically ignores for logistical reasons.

Abortion: bodily autonomy and social responsibility. A woman’s body belongs to her—this is not a declaration, but a foundational principle of the system. The partner has no right of veto: the decision to terminate a pregnancy is made by the woman; the system ensures access without barriers and without stigma. Before the fetus is viable outside the mother’s body, this is an absolute right—a medical procedure requiring no justification to anyone.

Once this point is reached—and GCP medicine will gradually lower this threshold as surrogacy technologies advance—the situation changes fundamentally. If GCP is capable of delivering the fetus without the mother’s involvement, and if the removal procedure is medically safe for the woman, the fetus is not destroyed—it is transferred for artificial gestation. This is not a restriction on a woman’s right to terminate a pregnancy: she retains that right. It is a recognition that a viable human being has the right to life regardless of the parents’ decision.

Responsible conception. The system ensures access to contraception, genetic counseling, and comprehensive information about the consequences of childbearing—without barriers. Conception in a GCP society must be a conscious decision, not an accident. If a pregnancy results from a voluntary and mutual decision by both partners, and both subsequently decide against having the child—the reputation system records this as a socially significant fact: a reduction in reputational weight in the area of social responsibility. Not a criminal punishment—but a consequence of a conscious choice visible to society. This does not apply to cases of violence, psychological coercion, or medical indications—in such cases, the system does not record any social penalty: the responsibility does not lie with the victim.

# 14.2. The Early Years: The Neurophysiological Foundation

The first 1,000 days of life—a period of maximum neuroplasticity and critical importance for the formation of cognitive and emotional architecture.

Relieving parents of daily pressures. Routine tasks (meals, cleaning, logistics) are fully automated. Parents’ cognitive resources are freed up for quality interaction with the child. “Functional orphanhood”—a phenomenon where parents are physically present but emotionally drained—disappears.

Collective care is the norm, not a fallback. The hub is a community, not a communal apartment. Childcare within it is organized on the principle of a village: early childhood development specialists, educators, and other hub members participate in the child’s life from the very first weeks. A parent is not required to be present constantly—they can return to the project a day after giving birth if they wish, or devote several years to the child if they choose to do so. Both choices are equally valid. There is no penalty for “insufficient presence”: the reputation system evaluates the quality of interaction when it occurs, not the number of hours spent nearby. The child is not left without attention—they are simply without a specific parent at a specific moment, which are fundamentally different things.

Biometric support. Parents have access to data on the child’s condition (sleep patterns, stress marker levels). The system does not control, but informs: it suggests adjustments to the environment or routine before a crisis arises.

Early diagnosis. Modern neuroscience shows that addressing neurodifferences during the period of maximum neuroplasticity (ages 0–3) is significantly more effective than addressing them at school age. Pediatricians and developmental specialists working at the hub use observational data and standardized diagnostic tools to identify potential neurodifferences at an early stage—when intervention is most effective.

Childcare is not a private family matter, nor is it invisible labor that the system overlooks. At the hub, it is a full-fledged contribution to the reputation system on par with any other productive labor. A parent raising a child produces what Marx called the reproduction of the labor force—but more precisely: produces a human being as the primary productive force of the new system. The GCP explicitly accounts for this contribution: the time and quality of care are reflected in the reputation profile just as engineering or teaching contributions are. This is not charity on the part of the system—it is recognition of what was invisible under capitalism.

# 14.3. Hereditary Disorders and the Ethics of Intervention

The cybernetic system takes a fundamentally different approach to congenital disorders:

Genetic counseling. During the family planning stage, couples are informed about genetic risks. Preimplantation genetic testing for monogenic disorders (PGT-M) becomes a standard, rather than an elite, option.

In utero correction. When pathologies are detected in the early stages of pregnancy, CRISPR technologies are used for precision editing. Automated fetal surgery corrects developmental defects with a precision unattainable by human hands.

Environmental adaptation. For conditions that cannot be corrected, the principle is this: “disability” is not a human trait, but a defect in the environment. If the environment adapts to the person (exoskeletons, neural interfaces, adapted hub architecture), the condition ceases to be a social sentence.

The “triple-check” ethical protocol. Any genetic intervention undergoes triple verification: a medical check (objective confirmation of the pathology), a public check (a random sample of citizens with high standing), and a GCP algorithmic check (analysis of the consequences for the population’s gene pool).

The ban on “designer babies”—altering appearance, cognitive traits, or temperament at the parents’ request—is absolute. Only the elimination of objective pathologies is permitted. If we allow “improvement” based on a single standard, we will create a new form of biological inequality.

The ban on editing higher cognitive functions is justified on different grounds: it deprives a person of the freedom of self-determination. Intelligence develops through education and the environment—not through reprogramming an embryo.

# 14.4. Childhood: Education Without Alienation

Ages 0–3: Primary neurophysiological configuration.

The child explores the environment through touch, movement, and social interaction. The hub environment is designed to stimulate this exploration in a safe manner. Interaction with digital systems at this age is minimal: the priority is bodily experience and live human communication.

Ages 3–7: Learning through play.

At this age, the prefrontal cortex—the center of self-control and planning—develops. The pedagogy is based on the theories of Piaget and Vygotsky: the zone of proximal development, learning through action.

The hubs create an environment where children work with real (but safe) tools: hydroponics, basic robotics, and 3D modeling. The direct link between effort and its result is fundamentally important: a child who has grown a plant or assembled a mechanism receives an immediate, tangible result of their activity—eliminating alienation at its very root.

“Reputation” at this age is not a number, but a level of trust. A child who works responsibly with tools gains access to more complex tools. Destructive behavior limits this access—not as a “punishment,” but as a direct consequence of a breach of trust.

Ages 7–12: Complexity and Specialization.

The GCP begins to collect data on the child’s cognitive characteristics—a tendency toward abstract or applied thinking, the pace of skill acquisition, and patterns of social interaction. Not to label or correct a “deviation”—but to offer an environment and tasks that correspond to exactly how this individual perceives the world. The roadmap of possibilities is not a route that must be followed.

The principle of knowledge construction at this age is fundamentally different from that of traditional schooling. Traditional logic goes: facts first, then meaning—memorize cell structure, then learn why mitochondria exist. This contradicts how the brain actually absorbs knowledge: new information sticks only if it’s embedded into an existing structure. Without structure, facts fall away.

The Hub’s pedagogy is built on the reverse order: big ideas first, then the details as their concrete manifestations. Evolution, as the central organizing idea of biology, is studied before anatomy—because only through it does it become clear why each specific structure exists. Systems thinking—the understanding that everything is connected to everything else—is formed before specialization, because without it, specialization breeds professional blindness. The history of science is studied in parallel with science itself—so that knowledge does not appear as a set of ready-made answers, but rather so that it is clear how questions arise and how they are answered.

This is not an abstract pedagogical principle—it is supported by modern cognitive science. Research on “threshold concepts” shows that there are ideas whose understanding opens up entire fields of knowledge. Evolution is one of them. Entropy is another. The principle of relativity is a third. When such an idea is deeply understood, everything studied afterward falls into place. When it is missing, knowledge remains a collection of unrelated facts.

Learning moves from the general to the specific: first, universal organizing ideas; then, the basic concepts of science, mathematics, engineering, and the humanities as their development; and only then specialization—based on the child’s own interests, with the system offering increasingly complex tasks in the chosen direction.

Ages 12–14: Initiation.

The adolescent brain undergoes rapid development of the limbic system while the prefrontal cortex is still maturing. This creates a specific state: a high drive for risk and novelty coupled with immature self-control.

The hub system offers the teenager “trainee” status and the right to make a real contribution to the infrastructure—with a safety net, not in a simulator. A fundamental difference: the teenager works with real equipment and real tasks—their decisions have real consequences within the hub. An error in the vertical farm’s control code won’t destroy the harvest—the system will intercept the critical action before execution—but it will delay automatic watering for three hours, and everyone will see it. Not a simulator, but training with a parachute: the jump is real, the ground is real, but the height is chosen so that one can learn from the consequences without a catastrophe. This is precisely what makes the task interesting—the stakes are real, not just theoretical. Mobility—the opportunity to participate in projects at other hubs or on expeditions—unlocks as the reputation index accumulates. The choice remains with the teenager.

By age 14, based on accumulated data about the teenager’s interests and contributions, the system generates a map of opportunities—not an assignment, but a suggestion: “Your experience and inclinations align well with systems biology or climate cluster management.” The teenager can choose a different path—and receive an adaptive training plan to fill in the missing competencies.

# 14.5. Physical Development: The Kinetic City

In a world where heavy manual labor has been replaced by automation, there is a risk of physical inactivity. The hub’s architecture addresses this not through coercion, but through environmental design.

Kinetic architecture. Hubs are designed so that active movement—climbing walls, parkour courses, active navigation systems—is the most convenient and engaging way to move within the space. Movement is integrated into daily life.

AR sports. Augmented reality transforms physical activity into an interactive quest. Motion sensors and an AR interface create a gaming context around running, swimming, and team sports. Dopamine reinforcement is triggered not at the end of a workout, but during the process—eliminating the psychological barrier.

Reputation and physical status. A physical fitness index is a prerequisite for access to certain activities: work in extreme conditions (high altitudes, deep-sea exploration, space) requires verified physical status. This creates a natural motivation to stay in shape—not out of fear, but out of a desire to tackle more challenging tasks.

Medical monitoring is voluntary. Those who wish to do so can track their own metrics (HRV, lactate, other markers) using the hub’s medical center equipment and adjust their workouts based on real-time data about their condition. This prevents overtraining and injuries—but the decision to monitor remains up to the individual.

Multi-layered pacing. The Kinetic Hub is not a one-size-fits-all rhythm. The parkour course and the quiet garden coexist in the same space because the hub is designed for the entire human life cycle, not just for the young and healthy. An elderly person with different sensory sensitivity, a different energy reserve, and a different need for quiet is not an exception to the architecture, but one of its users. Rest areas, slow routes, and spaces with low acoustic load are not a concession to weakness, but a recognition that different ages require different environments.

# 14.6. Adult Life: The Mission Exchange

An adult in the GCP system is neither a “worker” nor a “consumer.” They are an agent of development, participating in society by choosing tasks that match their abilities and interests.

Mission Exchange. Based on data from the sensor network regarding the state of infrastructure, ecosystems, and social needs, an open list of tasks requiring human attention is generated. An individual sees tasks corresponding to their reputation level and competencies. The choice is theirs: a complex research mission in the Arctic or educational work in an urban hub.

Meritocratic Governance. The GCP is not a dictator. Complex strategic and ethical decisions—such as the direction of regional reclamation, priorities in medical research, and the architecture of the new hub—are put to a distributed vote. The weight of a vote is determined by one’s reputation profile in the relevant field: the opinion of a biology expert carries more weight on ecosystem issues. This is not elitism—it is information hygiene.

The Economy of Novelty. Since basic comfort is guaranteed, novelty becomes the primary “currency.” The creation of new knowledge, technology, or cultural meaning is the highest contribution to the system, and it is accordingly rewarded with expanded access to resources for the next, larger-scale project.

# 14.7. Old Age: Knowledge Transfer and Autonomous Intimacy

Retiring from active work in old age does not mean social marginalization. In a society where the primary value is knowledge and experience, rather than physical productivity, the elderly person occupies a natural position as a guardian and transmitter.

The Role of a Mentor. A reputation system makes people with accumulated expertise visible to those following a similar path—and creates a natural mechanism for knowledge transfer. This is not “retirement” in the sense of isolation—it is a change of role.

The biology of aging and the environment. An older person’s need for peace and quiet is neither a whim nor a weakness. It is a biological fact: with age, sensory sensitivity, information processing speed, and the energy reserve for filtering out external noise all change. What is perceived as invigorating dynamism at twenty may feel like chaos at seventy. This is not a disease—it is a different mode of operation for the nervous system.

But here it is important to distinguish between what is a biological reality and what is a product of capitalism. Under capitalism, the urban environment is designed for the “active consumer”: fast, mobile, and solvent. An older person is structurally marginalized in such an environment—not because they are old, but because the environment was not created for them. The desire to “get away from the young people” is often not a preference but a forced reaction to an environment that does not see you.

In the hub, this reason disappears. The environment is designed for the entire life cycle—including quiet zones, slow routes, and spaces with a different pace. But isolation is neither imposed nor encouraged: complete isolation leads to social stagnation, an obsession with illness, and accelerated decline—this is a finding of gerontology, independent of the economic system.

The principle of “autonomous closeness.” The optimal environment for an older person is neither isolation nor forced assimilation into the general flow. It is the opportunity to have one’s own quiet corner while remaining engaged in the life of the hub on one’s own terms. Intergenerational exchange—the transfer of skills, collaboration, and lively communication—does not occur on a schedule or out of obligation, but rather through natural attraction: the young person gains access to experience that cannot be conveyed through text, while the older person receives an influx of new energy and relevant contexts. GCP recognizes this exchange and factors it into the reputation profiles of both participants.

Care is the hub’s responsibility, not just the family’s. The model of “children must care for their parents” works in a society where there is no alternative. In the hub, there is an alternative. Caring for an elderly person is a professional activity with reputational weight, undertaken by the hub’s specialists. This is not a replacement for family care—it is the removal of its status as the only possible option. If adult children want to and are able to provide care, the hub supports this and counts it as a contribution. If they do not want to or cannot, the person is not left abandoned.

A difficult person has the right to dignified care. An elderly person who has fallen out with everyone, had a difficult temperament all their life, and is left alone—is not deprived of medical care, housing, or humane treatment. This is not a reward for good behavior—it is a fundamental principle of the system, established beyond the scope of any vote. Palliative care specialists with a strong reputation in social psychology work, among other things, with cases where there is no family or where relationships have broken down.

Palliative care. The end of life is accompanied by the highest level of medical and psychological support. Pain and cognitive changes are monitored proactively—not when they become unbearable, but while help is still possible. The quality of the final years of life is viewed as a systemic value—not as a cost.

Preservation of ideas. A person’s cognitive and cultural contributions are preserved in the hub’s digital archives and the global network—not as museum exhibits, but as active resources: ideas, methods, and created works continue to participate in the life of society.

# 14.8. Preventive homeostasis: medicine before illness

Medicine in the capitalist system is structurally oriented toward treatment, not prevention. This is not the result of doctors’ malice—it is a consequence of economic logic: illness generates demand for services, while health does not. The pharmaceutical market monetizes symptoms, not their absence. The insurance model reimburses treatment but rarely preventive interventions, since the payback period for prevention exceeds the insurer’s financial planning horizon.

GCP completely eliminates this structural imbalance: the system is interested in human health, not in treatment, since a sick person means a reduction in contributions to the system and an increase in resource consumption. For the first time in the history of medicine, the objective function aligns with human interests.

The principle of preventive homeostasis is built on four levels of monitoring—from population-wide to individual.

Level Zero—population-level monitoring via the hub’s infrastructure. The hub’s sewer network serves as a continuous source of data on the health of the entire community. Wastewater epidemiology—a field that, during the COVID-19 pandemic, demonstrated the ability to detect outbreaks four to seven days before the first clinical cases appeared—takes on a new dimension within the hub environment. Sensors in the sewer network continuously analyze biomarkers: viral load, markers of inflammation, antibiotic-resistant bacteria, and the population’s hormonal profile. This is not surveillance of a specific individual—the data is aggregated and anonymous by definition. It’s a thermometer for the entire hub: the system detects that the population as a whole is beginning to experience stress, that an outbreak of respiratory infection is approaching, that anxiety levels have risen—and reacts preemptively, before people start getting sick and seeking help. This level of monitoring requires no participation or consent from any individual—it operates through infrastructure that everyone uses anyway.

The first level is accessible biochemical monitoring at the individual’s request. The hub’s medical center provides regular diagnostics—not only of classic stress markers (cortisol, HRV), but also of the “gut-brain axis”: microbiome composition, short-chain fatty acid levels, and markers of intestinal permeability. Modern neurogastroenterology has convincingly demonstrated that the state of the microbiome directly influences anxiety, cognitive function, and immune status. This is a bidirectional relationship: chronic stress damages the microbiome, and a degraded microbiome exacerbates stress. The patient receives data about their condition and recommendations—the decision to adjust their nutritional plan remains theirs.

The second level is biomechanical monitoring. Chronic musculoskeletal disorders are one of the leading causes of reduced quality of life and work capacity. Their common feature is that they accumulate over years through micro-injuries and compensatory patterns that a person does not notice until pain appears. The hub’s sensor network tracks biomechanics in real time: joint load patterns, muscle tone, and spinal alignment during various activities. Upon detecting persistent compensatory patterns, the system adjusts the environment—the height and angle of the work surface, lighting parameters, acoustics, and the frequency and nature of suggested movement breaks—before symptoms appear.

The third level is preventive ergonomics. The hub’s environment is not static but adaptive: it adjusts to the physiological state of a specific person at a specific moment. A decrease in heart rate variability combined with increased muscle tension in the neck and shoulder area signals a need for a gentle adjustment to the regimen: a suggestion for physical activity, a change in the nature of the task, or an adjustment to the microclimate. Medicine of the future in the GCP is not about treating disease, but about eliminating the physical and environmental conditions that cause it.

A fundamental ethical boundary remains: the system informs and adapts the environment, but does not coerce. A person sees data about their own condition and the system’s recommendations—the decision remains theirs. Bodily autonomy is an absolute priority.

# Chapter 15. The Transition to the Neurosocium

This chapter differs fundamentally from the previous ones in terms of its temporal horizon. Everything described above—the GCP, the reputation economy, the kinetic city, the reorganization of the life cycle—falls within the scope of a technologically predictable future: the next 50–100 years, provided there is a conscious transition. The neurosocium is different. It is neither a project nor a forecast. It is a thought experiment exploring where the logic of human-AI integration might lead over the course of several centuries—if it leads anywhere at all. The neurophysiological and technological barriers standing in the way are insurmountable today—and it is unknown whether they are surmountable in principle. We include this section not as a plan, but as an honest indication of the vector: where the arrow points if it is not stopped.

# 15.1. Humans and AI: Partnership or Merger?

As the integration of humans with AI systems deepens, an unavoidable question arises: where is the line between “humans using AI” and “humans merging with AI”?

A possible trajectory: as AI interfaces become increasingly organic (neurointerfaces, direct interaction with computational resources), people may discover that they are capable of utilizing not only external GCP but also the cognitive resources of other people—pooling the computational power of biological neural networks in real time.

This is the “neurosocium”: a form of collective intelligence in which the boundaries between “my” and “shared” knowledge may begin to blur. Each person likely retains a local focus of consciousness (sensory input from their own body), but may gain direct access to a “shared computational cloud” (the memory and analytical capabilities of others).

# 15.2. Risks of Integration

This scenario carries specific risks:

Groupthink. If the cognitive processes of several people are synchronized, there is a risk of losing individual variability—the source of novelty and innovation.

Erosion of agency. With sufficiently deep integration, the question arises: who is the agent of the decision—me or the network?

Biological barrier. The brain is evolutionarily tuned for individual survival. Processing “others’” intentions as one’s own will apparently require profound neurobiological changes—whether voluntary or technologically enabled.

# 15.3. “The Right to Unpredictability”

The key principle that preserves the dialectic of development in a highly organized society is the right to unpredictability—the right to make choices that are not optimal from the algorithm’s perspective, the right to make mistakes, to take an irrational leap, to redefine oneself.

This is not a “right to harm others”—that remains restricted. It is the right to an unpredictable trajectory of personal development. A system that predicts every step a person takes and optimizes their path down to the last neuron risks turning development into the “unfolding of a pre-determined program,” potentially stifling the very essence of development.

Dialectics requires contradiction. A fully harmonized society is thermodynamic death. The “right to unpredictability” must be built into the GCP algorithm as a core value, explicitly prioritized over efficiency.

# 15.4. The Right to Offline: Analog Existence as a Sovereign Choice

The logic of the neurosocium, even in its mildest form—the gradual expansion of AI interfaces—gives rise to an asymmetry that must be explicitly acknowledged: the integrated individual gains a cognitive advantage over the non-integrated individual. If this asymmetry is not compensated for architecturally, it reproduces class inequality in a new form—not through ownership of the means of production, but through access to the means of knowledge.

The right to go offline is neither Luddism nor nostalgia. It is a logical consequence of the principle that the book advocates from its very first pages: the free development of each individual as a condition for the free development of all. If a person chooses to exist without neural interfaces, without constant biometric monitoring, without cognitive integration with the GCP—the system is obligated to ensure them a full life within the framework of a biologically autonomous body. Not a second-class life, not an existence on the periphery, but equal access to basic goods, respect, and opportunities for development.

This echoes the “right to opt out” from Chapter 12—but goes beyond it. The right to opt out describes territorial autonomy for groups that reject the system entirely. The right to go offline describes an individual’s choice regarding the degree of integration within the system: a person can use the GCP via a text interface and opt out of the neural interface—and this should not limit their access to resources or reputational opportunities.

In practice, this means that all system functions must be accessible via analog or minimally invasive interfaces. Biometric monitoring is voluntary. Cognitive integration is optional. The hub’s architecture is designed so that a person without a single digital device can obtain food, medical care, participate in voting, and contribute to the common cause.

Compensatory interfaces. Formal equality of rights is insufficient if the cognitive asymmetry between integrated and non-integrated people grows functionally. Historical experience shows that the denial of key communication tools leads to marginalization even with formally equal rights. To prevent this, the GCP supports the institution of compensatory interfaces—specialized assistants (both software agents and human intermediaries with the appropriate reputation)—that provide offline citizens with equivalent—albeit slower—access to information, resources, and governance processes. This is not the system’s condescension toward the “laggards”—it is an architectural obligation stemming from the principle of a level playing field as it applies to lifestyle choices.

Dialectically, this is not a contradiction of the system, but its strength. The presence of people choosing an analog existence preserves the necessary diversity of cognitive strategies within the system and protects against the systemic fragility that total digital dependence would create. The “right to unpredictability” from Section 15.3 applies not only to the trajectory of development but also to the way of being in the world.

# AFTERWORD. The Morning That Is Already Beginning

Chapter 0 described the morning. Tomatoes on a vertical farm, bread from the bakery, a day without an alarm clock—not because there’s no need to get up, but because there’s no reason to fear what will happen if you don’t. This morning exists for now only as an image.

But the seed is already in the ground.

Mondragon feeds 80,000 families without a capitalist standing between labor and the result. Rojava organizes production and self-governance under conditions of war and blockade. Open AI models run on standard hardware without licensing fees to corporations. Cooperative federations in dozens of countries are accumulating the legal and organizational experience that will be needed. Meshtastic is building a connection that cannot be turned off with the push of a button.

All of this exists separately. The task is to see the pattern and name it.

This book is not a party platform or a manual for revolution. It is an attempt to name what is already happening—and to show where it leads if we do not stop.

The specific next step for the reader is simple to the point of banality: find the nearest structure that is already operating according to this logic—a cooperative, a union, a mutual aid group, a municipal project—and join it. Not because this will immediately change the world. Because this is precisely how that critical mass is built up, without which the pattern remains invisible.

Marx wrote that philosophers have merely interpreted the world—the point is to change it. This book interprets. The change is up to you.

GCP — Global Cybernetic Planner: a decentralized, distributed resource management network based on the BFT protocol

OCC (Organic Composition of Capital) — the organic composition of capital (c/v): the ratio of constant capital to variable capital

TRPF (Tendency of the Rate of Profit to Fall) — the tendency of the rate of profit to fall: a structural law of capitalism arising from the growth of OCC

Active constant capital — a form of constant capital (c) that not only transfers value but also independently generates new combinations (self-learning AI)

Cognitive rent — the appropriation of the results of humanity’s collective intellectual labor by those who control the computing infrastructure

Cognitive cashback — the partial return of appropriated collective knowledge to public circulation through the use of AI to create a socially useful product

BFT (Byzantine Fault Tolerance) — a fault-tolerance protocol: the system continues to operate even if up to one-third of the nodes fail

Hub — the smallest autonomous unit of cybernetic civilization: a self-sufficient structure for 500–2,000 people with a production core, vertical farms, and a medical center

Atomic distribution — the principle of GCP logistics: it is not the goods that are moved, but the production file; only raw materials are physically transported

Reputational capital — a multidimensional vector of an individual’s verified contribution, convertible into priority access to resources beyond the basic level

Preventive homeostasis — the principle of GCP medicine: eliminating the physical and environmental conditions of disease before it arises

Neurosocium — a form of collective intelligence based on the direct cognitive integration of people (thought experiment, centuries-long horizon)

Kinetic city — urban architecture that integrates physical activity into everyday navigation as the norm
